## Module 1: Advanced Pediatric Physiology and Pathophysiology (12 hours)

### Lesson 1.1: Cellular and Molecular Basis of Shock
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Interactive

Understanding cellular mechanisms of shock guides advanced management strategies.

**Cellular Physiology:**
- Aerobic metabolism: Glucose → Pyruvate → Acetyl-CoA → ATP (38 ATP per glucose)
- Anaerobic metabolism: Glucose → Pyruvate → Lactate (2 ATP per glucose)
- Mitochondrial dysfunction in shock
- Cellular apoptosis and necrosis
- Inflammatory cascade activation

**Shock Cascade:**
- Initial hypoperfusion
- Cellular hypoxia
- Switch to anaerobic metabolism
- Lactate accumulation
- Acidosis
- Cellular dysfunction
- Organ failure
- Death if not reversed

**Biomarkers of Shock:**
- Lactate (tissue hypoxia marker)
- Base deficit (metabolic acidosis)
- ScvO2 (central venous oxygen saturation)
- Procalcitonin (inflammatory marker)
- Troponin (myocardial injury)

**Interactive Element:** Animated cellular metabolism and shock cascade with learner control.

The neuroendocrine system responds to shock with complex compensatory mechanisms.

**Sympathetic Nervous System:**

- Increased heart rate and contractility
- Vasoconstriction
- Bronchodilation
- Inhibition of digestion

**Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal Axis:**
- Corticotropin-releasing hormone (CRH)
- Adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH)
- Cortisol release
- Gluconeogenesis
- Immunosuppression

**Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System:**
- Renin release
- Angiotensin II formation
- Vasoconstriction
- Aldosterone release
- Sodium and water retention

**Inflammatory Mediators:**
- Cytokines (TNF-α, IL-1, IL-6)
- Chemokines
- Complement activation
- Coagulation cascade activation

**Assessment:** Learner must identify neuroendocrine responses to different shock types.

### Lesson 1.4: Organ System Responses to Shock
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Assessment

Different organ systems respond differently to shock, with variable tolerance.

**Brain:**
- Most sensitive to hypoxia
- Cerebral autoregulation maintained until severe shock
- Altered mental status early sign of shock
- Cerebral edema in severe shock

**Heart:**
- Increased contractility initially
- Decreased contractility with prolonged shock
- Myocardial infarction possible
- Arrhythmias common

**Lungs:**
- Tachypnea compensates for acidosis
- Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS) in severe shock
- Pulmonary edema
- Ventilation-perfusion mismatch

**Kidneys:**
- Decreased renal perfusion
- Acute kidney injury
- Oliguria or anuria
- Hyperkalemia

**Liver:**
- Decreased hepatic perfusion
- Hepatic dysfunction
- Coagulopathy
- Encephalopathy

**Gut:**
- Bacterial translocation
- Sepsis risk
- Mesenteric ischemia

**Assessment:** Learner must identify organ-specific responses to shock.

### Lesson 1.5: Compensatory Mechanisms and Their Limits
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Case Study

Understanding compensatory mechanisms helps recognize when they are failing.

**Cardiovascular Compensation:**
- Increased heart rate
- Increased contractility
- Vasoconstriction
- Fluid shifts from interstitium to intravascular space

**Respiratory Compensation:**
- Increased respiratory rate
- Increased tidal volume
- Hyperventilation to blow off CO2

**Renal Compensation:**
- Fluid and sodium retention
- Decreased urine output
- Increased renal perfusion pressure

**Limits of Compensation:**
- Tachycardia cannot increase indefinitely
- Vasoconstriction cannot maintain perfusion if volume depleted
- Hyperventilation cannot compensate for severe acidosis
- Compensated shock can suddenly decompensate

**Case Study:** Shock progression from compensated to decompensated. Learner must recognize critical transition point.

## Module 2: Advanced Hemodynamic Monitoring (12 hours)

### Lesson 2.1: Invasive Hemodynamic Monitoring
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Assessment

Invasive monitoring provides detailed hemodynamic data guiding advanced management.

**Central Venous Pressure (CVP):**
- Normal: 2-8 mmHg
- Reflects right ventricular preload
- Elevated in: Right heart failure, tamponade, tension pneumothorax
- Decreased in: Hypovolemia

**Pulmonary Artery Catheter (Swan-Ganz):**
- Pulmonary artery pressure
- Pulmonary capillary wedge pressure (PCWP)
- Cardiac output measurement
- Mixed venous oxygen saturation (SvO2)

**Cardiac Output Calculation:**
- CO = (SaO2 - SvO2) × Hb × 10 / VO2
- Normal: 4-8 L/min
- Indexed to body surface area: CI = CO / BSA (normal 2.5-4 L/min/m²)

**Systemic Vascular Resistance:**
- SVR = (MAP - CVP) / CO × 80
- Normal: 800-1200 mmHg·min/L

**Assessment:** Learner must interpret hemodynamic parameters and identify shock type.

### Lesson 2.4: Lactate and Base Deficit Interpretation
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Assessment

Lactate and base deficit guide resuscitation and prognostication.

**Lactate Metabolism:**
- Produced during anaerobic metabolism
- Cleared by liver and kidneys
- Elevated in shock, sepsis, liver disease
- Lactate clearance reflects tissue perfusion

**Lactate Levels:**
- Normal: <2 mmol/L
- Elevated: 2-4 mmol/L (mild)
- Severe: >4 mmol/L

**Lactate Clearance:**
- Goal: >10% per hour
- Reflects resuscitation adequacy
- Persistent elevation indicates inadequate perfusion

**Base Deficit:**
- Reflects metabolic acidosis
- Normal: -2 to +2 mEq/L
- Negative values indicate acidosis
- Correlates with shock severity

**Assessment:** Learner must interpret lactate and base deficit and guide resuscitation.

### Lesson 2.5: Tissue Oxygenation Assessment
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Assessment

Assessing tissue oxygenation guides resuscitation endpoints.

**Oxygen Delivery (DO2):**
- DO2 = CO × CaO2
- CaO2 = (Hb × 1.34 × SaO2) + (PaO2 × 0.003)
- Normal: 500-600 mL/min/m²

**Oxygen Consumption (VO2):**
- VO2 = CO × (CaO2 - CvO2)
- Normal: 100-150 mL/min/m²

**Oxygen Extraction Ratio:**
- OER = (CaO2 - CvO2) / CaO2
- Normal: 25-30%
- Elevated in shock (>50%)

**ScvO2 (Central Venous Oxygen Saturation):**
- Normal: 70-75%
- <70% indicates inadequate oxygen delivery or increased consumption
- Goal in sepsis: >70%

**Assessment:** Learner must calculate and interpret tissue oxygenation parameters.

## Module 3: Advanced Airway Management (12 hours)

### Lesson 3.1: Difficult Airway Prediction and Management
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Assessment

Predicting and managing difficult airways prevents hypoxemia and aspiration.

**Difficult Airway Predictors:**
- Limited mouth opening (<3 cm)
- Micrognathia or retrognathia
- Macroglossia
- Cervical spine immobility
- Obesity
- Edema (angioedema, epiglottitis)
- Trauma

**Difficult Airway Algorithm:**
1. Assess airway
2. Plan approach
3. Prepare equipment
4. Attempt intubation
5. If failed, use alternative technique
6. Surgical airway if all else fails

**Alternative Techniques:**
- Video laryngoscopy
- Bougie-assisted intubation
- Blind nasal intubation
- Retrograde intubation
- Fiberoptic intubation
- Surgical airway

**Assessment:** Learner must predict difficult airways and select appropriate management.

---

### Lesson 3.2: Surgical Airway Techniques
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Hands-On

Surgical airway is the definitive airway when other techniques fail.

**Needle Cricothyrotomy:**
- Indications: Airway obstruction, cannot intubate or ventilate
- Technique: Needle through cricothyroid membrane
- Temporary measure only
- Requires definitive airway

**Surgical Cricothyrotomy:**
- Indications: Airway obstruction, cannot intubate or ventilate
- Technique: Incision through cricothyroid membrane
- More definitive than needle
- Complications: Tracheal stenosis, laryngeal injury

**Tracheostomy:**
- Indications: Prolonged airway need, airway obstruction above larynx
- Technique: Surgical incision below larynx
- Most definitive airway
- Requires operating room

**Hands-On Practice:** Learner practices surgical airway techniques on models.

---

### Lesson 3.3: Rapid Sequence Intubation
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Hands-On

Rapid sequence intubation minimizes aspiration risk in emergency situations.

**RSI Steps:**
1. **Preparation:** Gather equipment, position patient
2. **Preoxygenation:** 100% oxygen for 3-5 minutes
3. **Pretreatment:** Medications to prevent complications
4. **Paralysis and Induction:** Paralytic and induction agent
5. **Placement:** Insert endotracheal tube
6. **Post-intubation:** Confirm placement, secure tube

**Pretreatment Medications:**
- Atropine: Prevents bradycardia (especially in children)
- Lidocaine: Blunts airway reflexes
- Fentanyl: Blunts sympathetic response

**Induction Agents:**
- Etomidate: Minimal hemodynamic effects
- Ketamine: Maintains hemodynamics
- Propofol: Rapid onset, hemodynamic effects
- Thiopental: Rapid onset, hemodynamic effects

**Paralytic Agents:**
- Succinylcholine: Rapid onset, short duration
- Rocuronium: Slower onset, longer duration

**Hands-On Practice:** Learner practices RSI on manikins and simulations.

ECMO provides advanced respiratory and circulatory support for refractory shock and respiratory failure.

**ECMO Types:**

**Indications:**

**ECMO Circuit:**

**Management:**

- Sedation and analgesia
- Monitoring
- Weaning

**Assessment:** Learner must identify ECMO indications and understand management principles.

## Module 4: Advanced Pharmacology (12 hours)

### Lesson 4.1: Inotropes and Vasopressors
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Assessment

Inotropes and vasopressors support circulation in shock states.

**Inotropes:**
- **Dobutamine:** Beta-1 effects, increases contractility and heart rate
- **Milrinone:** Phosphodiesterase inhibitor, increases contractility and causes vasodilation
- **Levosimendan:** Calcium sensitizer (not available in US)

**Vasopressors:**
- **Norepinephrine:** Alpha and beta effects, preferred in sepsis
- **Dopamine:** Dose-dependent effects
- **Epinephrine:** Alpha and beta effects
- **Phenylephrine:** Pure alpha effects
- **Vasopressin:** Non-catecholamine vasopressor

**Dosing and Titration:**
- Start low, titrate to effect
- Goal: Adequate perfusion pressure
- Wean as shock resolves

**Assessment:** Learner must select appropriate inotropes and vasopressors for different shock types.

Appropriate sedation and analgesia improve patient comfort and outcomes.

**Sedatives:**

- **Midazolam:** Intermediate onset and duration
- **Lorazepam:** Longer duration

### Lesson 4.3: Antibiotics in Sepsis
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Assessment

Appropriate antibiotic selection and timing are critical in sepsis.

**Antibiotic Selection:**
- Empiric broad-spectrum coverage
- Based on likely pathogens and local resistance patterns
- Narrowed based on culture results

**Common Regimens:**
- **Community-acquired:** Ceftriaxone + azithromycin
- **Hospital-acquired:** Piperacillin-tazobactam or carbapenem
- **Immunocompromised:** Broader coverage

**Timing:**
- Goal: Within 1 hour of recognition
- Earlier administration improves outcomes

**Dosing:**
- Higher doses in sepsis
- Adjust for renal function
- Monitor levels if indicated

**Assessment:** Learner must select appropriate antibiotics and dosing for different infections.

### Lesson 4.4: Corticosteroids in Shock
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Assessment

Corticosteroids may improve outcomes in specific shock states.

**Septic Shock:**
- Low-dose hydrocortisone (50 mg IV every 6 hours)
- Consider if refractory hypotension
- May improve outcomes

**Anaphylaxis:**
- Epinephrine first-line
- Corticosteroids after epinephrine
- Prevent biphasic reaction

**Adrenal Insufficiency:**
- High-dose hydrocortisone (50 mg IV every 6 hours)
- Critical in adrenal crisis

**Assessment:** Learner must identify appropriate corticosteroid use in different shock states.

---

### Lesson 4.5: Antiarrhythmic Medications
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Assessment

Antiarrhythmic medications treat arrhythmias in cardiac arrest and post-resuscitation.

**Class I (Sodium Channel Blockers):**
- Quinidine, Procainamide, Disopyramide, Lidocaine
- Slow conduction
- Decrease automaticity

**Class II (Beta-Blockers):**
- Propranolol, Metoprolol, Esmolol
- Slow AV nodal conduction
- Decrease heart rate

**Class III (Potassium Channel Blockers):**
- Amiodarone, Sotalol, Ibutilide
- Prolong action potential
- Preferred in cardiac arrest

**Class IV (Calcium Channel Blockers):**
- Verapamil, Diltiazem
- Slow AV nodal conduction
- Useful for SVT

**Assessment:** Learner must select appropriate antiarrhythmic for different arrhythmias.

## Module 5: Advanced Resuscitation Techniques (12 hours)

### Lesson 5.1: Extracorporeal CPR (ECPR)
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Assessment

ECPR provides advanced support for refractory cardiac arrest.

**Indications:**
- Refractory cardiac arrest
- Reversible cause (PE, MI, hypothermia)
- Available ECMO capability
- Young patient

**Technique:**
- Continue conventional CPR
- Initiate ECMO cannulation
- Transition to ECMO support
- Treat underlying cause

**Outcomes:**
- Better outcomes than conventional CPR alone
- Requires rapid ECMO team activation
- Limited availability

**Assessment:** Learner must identify ECPR candidates and understand management.

---

### Lesson 5.2: Resuscitative Hysterotomy
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Assessment

Perimortem cesarean section improves outcomes in pregnant cardiac arrest.

**Indications:**
- Cardiac arrest in pregnant woman
- No ROSC after 4 minutes of CPR
- Fundus above umbilicus (viable pregnancy)

**Technique:**
- Vertical midline incision
- Rapid delivery of fetus
- Relieves aortocaval compression
- Improves maternal and fetal outcomes

**Timing:**
- Perform within 5 minutes of cardiac arrest
- Do not delay for transport
- Perform in hospital if possible

**Assessment:** Learner must identify need for resuscitative hysterotomy.

## Module 6: Advanced Pediatric Emergencies (12 hours)

### Lesson 6.1: Pediatric Sepsis and Septic Shock
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Case Study

Sepsis is a leading cause of death in children worldwide.

**Sepsis Recognition:**
- Fever or hypothermia
- Tachycardia
- Tachypnea
- Altered mental status
- Skin changes

**Septic Shock:**
- Hypotension despite fluid resuscitation
- Altered mental status
- Signs of poor perfusion

**Management:**
- Early recognition
- Rapid fluid resuscitation (60 mL/kg in first hour)
- Broad-spectrum antibiotics
- Vasopressors if needed
- Source control

**Outcomes:**
- Early recognition and treatment improve outcomes
- Delays in treatment increase mortality

**Case Study:** Pediatric sepsis progressing to shock. Learner must manage appropriately.

---

### Lesson 6.2: Pediatric Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS)
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Case Study

ARDS is a severe respiratory condition requiring advanced management.

**ARDS Criteria:**
- Acute onset (within 1 week)
- Bilateral infiltrates on imaging
- Respiratory failure not explained by cardiac failure
- PaO2/FiO2 ratio <300

**Severity:**
- Mild: 200-300
- Moderate: 100-200
- Severe: <100

**Management:**
- Lung-protective ventilation (6-8 mL/kg, PEEP)
- Prone positioning
- Neuromuscular blockade
- Sedation
- ECMO if refractory

**Case Study:** ARDS scenario. Learner must manage appropriately.

---

### Lesson 6.3: Pediatric Myocarditis and Cardiogenic Shock
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Case Study

Myocarditis can cause sudden cardiogenic shock and cardiac arrest.

**Recognition:**
- Fever, viral prodrome
- Chest pain
- Dyspnea
- Palpitations
- Syncope

**Diagnosis:**
- ECG changes
- Elevated troponin
- Echocardiography
- Cardiac MRI

**Management:**
- Supportive care
- Inotropes and vasopressors

**Case Study:** Myocarditis with cardiogenic shock. Learner must manage appropriately.

---

### Lesson 6.4: Pediatric Status Epilepticus
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Case Study

Status epilepticus is a medical emergency with risk of brain damage and death.

**Recognition:**
- Continuous seizures or multiple seizures without recovery
- Duration >5 minutes

**Management:**
- Safety
- Oxygen
- IV access
- Medications:
  - Lorazepam (0.1 mg/kg IV, max 4 mg)
  - Diazepam (0.1-0.3 mg/kg IV, max 10 mg)
  - Midazolam (0.1-0.2 mg/kg IM/IV)
- Second-line agents if no response
- Treat underlying cause
- Transport to hospital

**Refractory Status Epilepticus:**
- No response to first-line agents
- Requires intubation and sedation
- Anesthetic agents (propofol, pentobarbital)

**Case Study:** Status epilepticus scenario. Learner must manage appropriately.
