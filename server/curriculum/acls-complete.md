## Module 1: Advanced Cardiac Physiology (6 hours)

### Lesson 1.1: Cardiac Electrophysiology
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Interactive

Understanding cardiac electrophysiology is fundamental to recognizing and treating cardiac arrhythmias.

**Cardiac Action Potential:**
- **Phase 0 (Depolarization):** Rapid sodium influx, ventricular depolarization
- **Phase 1 (Early Repolarization):** Potassium efflux, sodium channel inactivation
- **Phase 2 (Plateau):** Calcium influx balances potassium efflux
- **Phase 3 (Repolarization):** Potassium efflux, membrane potential restoration
- **Phase 4 (Resting):** Sodium-potassium pump maintains resting potential

**Conduction System:**
- **SA Node:** Generates impulse (60-100 bpm)
- **Atrial Conduction:** Spreads through atria (Bachmann's bundle, internodal pathways)
- **AV Node:** Delays conduction (100-120 ms)
- **His Bundle:** Rapid conduction
- **Purkinje Fibers:** Rapid ventricular conduction

**Refractory Periods:**
- **Absolute Refractory Period:** No stimulus can generate action potential
- **Relative Refractory Period:** Stronger stimulus can generate action potential
- **Supernormal Period:** Weaker stimulus can generate action potential

**Interactive Element:** Animated cardiac action potential with learner control of ion channels.

### Lesson 1.2: ECG Interpretation Basics
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Assessment

Interpreting electrocardiograms (ECGs) is essential for recognizing cardiac arrhythmias.

**ECG Paper:**
- **Horizontal Axis:** Time (1 mm = 0.04 seconds, 5 mm = 0.2 seconds)
- **Vertical Axis:** Voltage (1 mm = 0.1 mV, 10 mm = 1 mV)

**ECG Waves and Intervals:**
- **P Wave:** Atrial depolarization (0.08-0.12 seconds)
- **PR Interval:** SA node to ventricular depolarization (0.12-0.20 seconds)
- **QRS Complex:** Ventricular depolarization (0.08-0.12 seconds)
- **ST Segment:** Ventricular repolarization onset
- **T Wave:** Ventricular repolarization
- **QT Interval:** Ventricular depolarization and repolarization (0.36-0.44 seconds)

**Normal ECG:**
- Rate: 60-100 bpm
- Rhythm: Regular
- P waves: Present, upright in II
- PR interval: 0.12-0.20 seconds
- QRS: 0.08-0.12 seconds
- ST segment: Isoelectric
- T wave: Upright in II

**Assessment:** Learner must identify normal ECG components and measure intervals.

### Lesson 1.3: Cardiac Arrhythmias Overview
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Interactive

Cardiac arrhythmias are disturbances in heart rate or rhythm. Classification and recognition are essential for appropriate treatment.

**Arrhythmia Classification:**

**Bradyarrhythmias (Rate <60 bpm):**
- Sinus bradycardia
- AV block
- Junctional rhythms
- Idioventricular rhythms

**Tachyarrhythmias (Rate >100 bpm):**
- Sinus tachycardia
- Atrial fibrillation
- Atrial flutter
- Supraventricular tachycardia
- Ventricular tachycardia
- Ventricular fibrillation

**Shockable Rhythms (Require Defibrillation):**
- Ventricular fibrillation (VF)
- Pulseless ventricular tachycardia (VT)

**Non-Shockable Rhythms (Do Not Require Defibrillation):**
- Asystole
- Pulseless electrical activity (PEA)

**Interactive Element:** ECG strips with arrhythmia identification and classification.

---

### Lesson 1.4: Coronary Artery Disease and Acute Coronary Syndrome
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Case Study

Acute coronary syndrome (ACS) is a leading cause of cardiac arrest. Recognition and treatment are time-critical.

**Pathophysiology:**
- Atherosclerotic plaque rupture
- Thrombus formation
- Coronary artery occlusion
- Myocardial ischemia
- Myocardial infarction

**Presentation:**
- Chest pain or pressure
- Dyspnea
- Diaphoresis
- Nausea
- Radiation to arm, neck, jaw
- May be silent (especially in women, elderly, diabetics)

**ECG Changes:**
- ST elevation (STEMI)
- ST depression (NSTEMI)
- T wave inversion
- Hyperkalemia changes
- Wellens' sign

**Treatment:**
- Oxygen
- Aspirin
- Nitroglycerin
- Heparin
- Clopidogrel or ticagrelor
- Reperfusion (PCI or thrombolytics)

**Case Study:** ACS presentation with ECG changes. Learner must recognize and manage appropriately.

---

### Lesson 1.5: Heart Failure and Pulmonary Edema
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Case Study

Heart failure is a common cause of respiratory distress and can progress to cardiogenic shock and cardiac arrest.

**Pathophysiology:**
- Systolic dysfunction (decreased contractility)
- Diastolic dysfunction (decreased compliance)
- Compensatory mechanisms (increased heart rate, vasoconstriction)
- Decompensation (pulmonary edema, cardiogenic shock)

**Presentation:**
- Dyspnea
- Orthopnea
- Paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea
- Fatigue
- Edema
- Pulmonary crackles
- Elevated JVP

**Acute Decompensated Heart Failure:**
- Acute dyspnea
- Pulmonary edema
- Cardiogenic shock
- Arrhythmias

**Treatment:**
- Oxygen
- Diuretics
- Vasodilators
- Inotropes
- ACE inhibitors
- Beta-blockers

**Case Study:** Acute heart failure with pulmonary edema. Learner must manage appropriately.

## Module 2: Cardiac Arrhythmia Recognition and Management (8 hours)

### Lesson 2.1: Bradycardia Recognition and Management
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Assessment

Bradycardia (heart rate <60 bpm in children, <100 bpm in infants) can be normal or pathological depending on context.

**Sinus Bradycardia:**
- Regular rhythm
- P waves present
- PR interval normal
- QRS normal
- Rate <60 bpm (children) or <100 bpm (infants)

**Causes:**
- Athletic conditioning
- Hypothermia
- Hypothyroidism
- Increased intracranial pressure
- Medication effects (beta-blockers, digoxin)
- Hypoxia
- Vagal stimulation

**Symptomatic Bradycardia:**
- Hypotension
- Altered mental status
- Signs of shock
- Chest pain
- Dyspnea

**Management:**
- **Asymptomatic:** Observation
- **Symptomatic:** 
  - Oxygen
  - IV access
  - Atropine (0.02 mg/kg IV, min 0.1 mg, max 0.5 mg)
  - Consider pacing
  - Treat underlying cause

**AV Block:**
- **1st Degree:** Prolonged PR interval (>0.20 seconds)
- **2nd Degree Type I (Wenckebach):** Progressive PR prolongation, dropped beat
- **2nd Degree Type II:** Fixed PR interval, dropped beats
- **3rd Degree (Complete):** No AV conduction, independent atrial and ventricular rates

**Assessment:** Learner must recognize bradycardia and determine appropriate management.

---

### Lesson 2.2: Tachycardia Recognition and Management
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Assessment

Tachycardia (heart rate >100 bpm in children, >160 bpm in infants) can be appropriate or pathological.

**Sinus Tachycardia:**
- Regular rhythm
- P waves present
- PR interval normal
- QRS normal
- Rate >100 bpm (children) or >160 bpm (infants)

**Causes:**
- Fever
- Pain
- Anxiety
- Hypovolemia
- Hypoxia
- Anemia
- Hyperthyroidism
- Medications

**Management:**
- Treat underlying cause
- Usually no specific treatment needed
- Observe for complications

**Supraventricular Tachycardia (SVT):**
- Regular, rapid rhythm
- P waves may be buried in QRS or T wave
- Rate 220-300 bpm (infants), 150-220 bpm (children)
- Abrupt onset and offset

**SVT Management:**
- **Stable:** Vagal maneuvers (ice to face in infants, Valsalva in older children)
- **Unstable:** Synchronized cardioversion (0.5-1 J/kg, then 2 J/kg)
- **Medication:** Adenosine (0.1 mg/kg IV rapid push, max 6 mg first dose, 12 mg second dose)

**Atrial Fibrillation:**
- Irregular rhythm
- No P waves (replaced by fibrillation waves)
- Variable ventricular rate
- Rare in children (consider underlying disease)

**Atrial Flutter:**
- Regular or irregular rhythm
- Sawtooth P waves (flutter waves)
- Regular ventricular rate if fixed AV block
- Variable ventricular rate if variable AV block

**Assessment:** Learner must recognize tachyarrhythmias and determine appropriate management.

---

### Lesson 2.3: Ventricular Fibrillation and Pulseless Ventricular Tachycardia
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Assessment

Ventricular fibrillation (VF) and pulseless ventricular tachycardia (VT) are shockable rhythms requiring immediate defibrillation.

**Ventricular Fibrillation:**
- Chaotic, disorganized rhythm
- No organized complexes
- No pulse
- Requires immediate defibrillation
- Most common initial rhythm in adult cardiac arrest
- Less common in pediatric cardiac arrest

**Pulseless Ventricular Tachycardia:**
- Regular, rapid rhythm
- Wide QRS complexes (>0.12 seconds)
- No pulse
- Requires immediate defibrillation

**Management:**
1. **Recognize:** Confirm VF/VT and pulselessness
2. **CPR:** Begin CPR immediately
3. **Defibrillation:** Deliver shock (2 J/kg, then 4 J/kg)
4. **CPR:** Resume CPR immediately
5. **Medications:** Epinephrine every 3-5 minutes
6. **Repeat:** Defibrillate every 2 minutes
7. **Advanced Airway:** Consider intubation
8. **Medications:** Consider amiodarone or lidocaine

**Defibrillation Technique:**
- Ensure pads have good contact
- Announce "Clear"
- Ensure no one touching patient
- Deliver shock
- Resume CPR immediately

**Assessment:** Learner must recognize VF/VT and manage appropriately.

---

### Lesson 2.4: Asystole and Pulseless Electrical Activity
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Assessment

Asystole and pulseless electrical activity (PEA) are non-shockable rhythms with poor prognosis but require specific management.

**Asystole:**
- Flat line on ECG
- No organized electrical activity
- No pulse
- No perfusion
- Poor prognosis
- Do not defibrillate

**Asystole Management:**
1. **CPR:** Begin CPR immediately
2. **Airway:** Establish advanced airway
3. **Medications:** Epinephrine every 3-5 minutes
4. **Rhythm Check:** Every 2 minutes
5. **Treat Cause:** Identify and treat reversible causes
6. **Consider:** Extracorporeal CPR if available

**Pulseless Electrical Activity (PEA):**
- Organized electrical activity on ECG
- No pulse
- No perfusion
- Poor prognosis
- Do not defibrillate

**PEA Management:**
1. **CPR:** Begin CPR immediately
2. **Airway:** Establish advanced airway
3. **Medications:** Epinephrine every 3-5 minutes
4. **Rhythm Check:** Every 2 minutes
5. **Treat Cause:** Identify and treat reversible causes
   - **H's:** Hypovolemia, Hypoxia, Hydrogen ion (acidosis), Hypothermia
   - **T's:** Tension pneumothorax, Tamponade, Thrombosis (PE), Toxins

**Reversible Causes:**
- Hypovolemia: Fluid resuscitation
- Hypoxia: Oxygen, ventilation
- Hypothermia: Rewarming
- Tension pneumothorax: Needle decompression
- Cardiac tamponade: Pericardiocentesis
- Thrombosis: Thrombolytics, embolectomy
- Toxins: Antidotes, dialysis

**Assessment:** Learner must recognize asystole and PEA and manage appropriately.

---

### Lesson 2.5: Post-Resuscitation Care
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Case Study

Management after return of spontaneous circulation (ROSC) is critical for survival and neurological outcome.

**Post-ROSC Stabilization:**
1. **Airway:** Secure airway if not already done
2. **Breathing:** Provide oxygen to maintain SpO2 94-98%
3. **Circulation:** Establish IV access, fluid resuscitation
4. **Monitoring:** Continuous cardiac monitoring
5. **ECG:** 12-lead ECG to identify ACS
6. **Temperature:** Maintain normothermia or mild hypothermia (32-34°C)
7. **Glucose:** Maintain 140-180 mg/dL
8. **Seizure Prophylaxis:** Consider if indicated

**Targeted Temperature Management:**
- Induce mild hypothermia (32-34°C) for 12-24 hours
- Rewarming at 0.5°C/hour
- Improves neurological outcome

**Medications:**
- Epinephrine: Continue if needed for hypotension
- Amiodarone: For recurrent arrhythmias
- Vasopressors: For hypotension
- Sedation: For intubated patients

**Transport:**
- Transport to appropriate hospital
- STEMI patients to PCI-capable center
- Cardiac arrest survivors to specialized centers if available

**Case Study:** Post-ROSC management scenario. Learner must stabilize and manage appropriately.

## Module 3: Advanced Airway Management (8 hours)

### Lesson 3.1: Endotracheal Intubation
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Hands-On

Endotracheal intubation provides definitive airway management with protection from aspiration.

**Indications:**
- Inability to maintain airway
- Inability to protect airway
- Inadequate ventilation
- Severe hypoxemia
- Need for prolonged ventilation

**Equipment:**
- Laryngoscope (direct or video)
- Endotracheal tubes (various sizes)
- Stylet
- Bougie
- Suction
- Medications (sedation, paralysis)

**Tube Sizing:**
- **Infants:** 3.0-3.5 mm
- **Toddlers:** 4.0-4.5 mm
- **Preschool:** 4.5-5.0 mm
- **School age:** 5.0-6.0 mm
- **Adolescents:** 6.0-7.0 mm

**Intubation Technique:**
1. **Preparation:** Gather equipment, position patient
2. **Pre-oxygenation:** Provide 100% oxygen
3. **Positioning:** Sniffing position (head extended, chin forward)
4. **Laryngoscopy:** Insert laryngoscope, visualize vocal cords
5. **Tube Placement:** Pass tube through vocal cords
6. **Confirmation:** Verify tube position (auscultation, capnography, chest X-ray)
7. **Securing:** Secure tube with tape or ties
8. **Monitoring:** Continuous monitoring of tube position

**Hands-On Practice:** Learner practices intubation on manikins.

---

### Lesson 3.2: Supraglottic Airway Devices
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Hands-On

Supraglottic airway devices (laryngeal mask airway, i-gel) provide alternative airway management.

**Advantages:**
- Easier insertion than intubation
- No need for paralysis
- Less airway trauma
- Rapid airway establishment

**Disadvantages:**
- Does not protect from aspiration
- May allow air leak
- Requires proper sizing
- Contraindicated in some situations

**Insertion Technique:**
1. **Preparation:** Gather equipment, position patient
2. **Pre-oxygenation:** Provide 100% oxygen
3. **Positioning:** Sniffing position
4. **Insertion:** Insert device following manufacturer instructions
5. **Confirmation:** Verify placement (auscultation, capnography)
6. **Securing:** Secure device
7. **Monitoring:** Continuous monitoring

**Hands-On Practice:** Learner practices insertion on manikins.

---

### Lesson 3.3: Difficult Airway Management
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Case Study

Difficult airway management requires alternative techniques and equipment.

**Predictors of Difficult Airway:**
- Limited mouth opening
- Micrognathia
- Macroglossia
- Cervical spine immobility
- Obesity
- Edema
- Trauma

**Alternative Techniques:**
- Video laryngoscopy
- Bougie-assisted intubation
- Blind nasal intubation
- Retrograde intubation
- Surgical airway

**Surgical Airway:**
- **Needle Cricothyrotomy:** Needle through cricothyroid membrane
- **Surgical Cricothyrotomy:** Incision through cricothyroid membrane
- **Tracheostomy:** Surgical airway below larynx

**Case Study:** Difficult airway scenario. Learner must manage appropriately.

---

### Lesson 3.4: Confirmation of Tube Placement
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Assessment

Confirming correct tube placement is critical to prevent hypoxemia and aspiration.

**Confirmation Methods:**

**Clinical Assessment:**
- Bilateral breath sounds
- Absence of epigastric sounds
- Chest rise with ventilation
- Condensation in tube

**Capnography:**
- Gold standard
- Waveform indicates correct placement
- Quantitative or qualitative

**Chest X-ray:**
- Confirms tube position
- Identifies complications
- Not real-time

**Esophageal Detector Device:**
- Detects air in esophagus
- Less reliable than capnography

**Assessment:** Learner must identify correct vs. incorrect tube placement.

---

### Lesson 3.5: Complications of Intubation
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Case Study

Intubation complications can occur during insertion, maintenance, or removal.

**Insertion Complications:**
- Dental trauma
- Lip/tongue trauma
- Esophageal intubation
- Right mainstem intubation
- Tube obstruction
- Sinusitis

**Maintenance Complications:**
- Tube obstruction
- Tube migration
- Sinusitis
- Ventilator-associated pneumonia
- Tracheal stenosis
- Laryngeal stenosis

**Removal Complications:**
- Stridor
- Laryngeal edema
- Vocal cord injury
- Tracheal stenosis

**Management:**
- Identify complication
- Reposition or replace tube
- Treat underlying cause
- Monitor for complications

**Case Study:** Intubation complication scenario. Learner must manage appropriately.

## Module 4: Medications in Cardiac Arrest (6 hours)

### Lesson 4.1: Epinephrine
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Assessment

Epinephrine is the primary medication in cardiac arrest, used for both shockable and non-shockable rhythms.

**Mechanism:**
- Alpha-adrenergic: Vasoconstriction, increased coronary perfusion pressure
- Beta-adrenergic: Increased heart rate, contractility

**Dosing:**
- **IV/IO:** 0.01 mg/kg (0.1 mL/kg of 1:10,000 solution) every 3-5 minutes
- **Endotracheal:** 0.1 mg/kg (0.1 mL/kg of 1:1,000 solution)

**Indications:**
- Asystole
- PEA
- Refractory VF/VT
- Bradycardia with hypotension
- Shock
- Anaphylaxis

**Adverse Effects:**
- Hypertension
- Tachycardia
- Arrhythmias
- Myocardial ischemia
- Anxiety

**Assessment:** Learner must identify appropriate epinephrine use and dosing.

---

### Lesson 4.2: Amiodarone and Lidocaine
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Assessment

Amiodarone and lidocaine are antiarrhythmic medications used for refractory VF/VT.

**Amiodarone:**
- **Mechanism:** Class III antiarrhythmic (potassium channel blocker)
- **Dosing:** 5 mg/kg IV/IO for VF/VT (max 300 mg first dose, 150 mg second dose)
- **Indications:** Refractory VF/VT, SVT, atrial fibrillation
- **Adverse Effects:** Hypotension, bradycardia, QT prolongation

**Lidocaine:**
- **Mechanism:** Class IB antiarrhythmic (sodium channel blocker)
- **Dosing:** 1 mg/kg IV/IO for VF/VT (max 100 mg)
- **Indications:** Refractory VF/VT, ventricular ectopy
- **Adverse Effects:** Seizures, hypotension, bradycardia

**Comparison:**
- Amiodarone preferred for refractory VF/VT
- Lidocaine alternative if amiodarone unavailable
- Both have similar efficacy

**Assessment:** Learner must identify appropriate antiarrhythmic use and dosing.

---

### Lesson 4.3: Sodium Bicarbonate and Calcium
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Assessment

Sodium bicarbonate and calcium are used for specific cardiac arrest situations.

**Sodium Bicarbonate:**
- **Mechanism:** Alkalinizes blood, counteracts acidosis
- **Dosing:** 1 mEq/kg IV/IO
- **Indications:** 
  - Tricyclic antidepressant overdose
  - Severe metabolic acidosis
  - Hyperkalemia
- **Adverse Effects:** Hypernatremia, alkalosis, hypokalemia

**Calcium:**
- **Mechanism:** Stabilizes cardiac membrane
- **Dosing:** 
  - Calcium chloride: 20 mg/kg IV/IO (max 2 g)
  - Calcium gluconate: 60 mg/kg IV/IO (max 6 g)
- **Indications:**
  - Hyperkalemia
  - Hypocalcemia
  - Calcium channel blocker overdose
  - Magnesium toxicity
- **Adverse Effects:** Tissue necrosis if extravasation, hyperkalemia

**Assessment:** Learner must identify appropriate use of sodium bicarbonate and calcium.

---

### Lesson 4.4: Vasopressors
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Assessment

Vasopressors are used to maintain blood pressure and perfusion in shock states.

**Dopamine:**
- **Mechanism:** Dose-dependent alpha and beta effects
- **Dosing:** 2-20 mcg/kg/min IV infusion
- **Indications:** Hypotension, shock
- **Adverse Effects:** Arrhythmias, myocardial ischemia, hypertension

**Norepinephrine:**
- **Mechanism:** Alpha and beta effects (primarily alpha)
- **Dosing:** 0.05-2 mcg/kg/min IV infusion
- **Indications:** Hypotension, shock, sepsis
- **Adverse Effects:** Arrhythmias, myocardial ischemia, hypertension

**Dobutamine:**
- **Mechanism:** Beta effects (primarily beta-1)
- **Dosing:** 2-20 mcg/kg/min IV infusion
- **Indications:** Cardiogenic shock, low cardiac output
- **Adverse Effects:** Tachycardia, arrhythmias, hypotension

**Assessment:** Learner must identify appropriate vasopressor use and dosing.

---

### Lesson 4.5: Other Medications
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Assessment

Other medications used in cardiac arrest and resuscitation.

**Atropine:**
- **Mechanism:** Anticholinergic, blocks vagal effects
- **Dosing:** 0.02 mg/kg IV/IO (min 0.1 mg, max 0.5 mg)
- **Indications:** Bradycardia with hypotension
- **Adverse Effects:** Tachycardia, arrhythmias, dry mouth

**Glucose:**
- **Mechanism:** Corrects hypoglycemia
- **Dosing:** 0.5-1 g/kg IV/IO (dextrose 25% or 50%)
- **Indications:** Documented or suspected hypoglycemia
- **Adverse Effects:** Hyperglycemia, thrombophlebitis if extravasation

**Naloxone:**
- **Mechanism:** Opioid antagonist
- **Dosing:** 0.1 mg/kg IV/IO (max 2 mg)
- **Indications:** Opioid overdose
- **Adverse Effects:** Withdrawal, agitation, pain

**Assessment:** Learner must identify appropriate use of other medications.

## Module 5: Special Resuscitation Situations (8 hours)

### Lesson 5.1: Resuscitation in Pregnancy
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Case Study

Pregnant women have unique physiological changes that affect resuscitation.

**Physiological Changes:**
- Increased oxygen consumption
- Decreased functional residual capacity
- Aortocaval compression
- Increased minute ventilation
- Altered drug metabolism

**Resuscitation Modifications:**
- **Position:** Left uterine displacement (left lateral tilt 15 degrees)
- **Compressions:** Standard technique, may need higher position due to enlarged uterus
- **Airway:** Anticipate difficult airway, have equipment ready
- **Medications:** Standard doses and timing
- **Defibrillation:** Safe in pregnancy, do not delay
- **Perimortem Cesarean:** Consider if no ROSC after 4 minutes

**Perimortem Cesarean Section:**
- Performed during resuscitation if no ROSC after 4 minutes
- Relieves aortocaval compression
- Improves maternal and fetal outcomes
- Performed by trained provider

**Case Study:** Pregnant woman with cardiac arrest. Learner must manage appropriately.

---

### Lesson 5.2: Resuscitation in Hypothermia
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Case Study

Hypothermia significantly alters resuscitation approach and outcomes.

**Hypothermia Classification:**
- **Mild (32-35°C):** Shivering, confusion, ataxia
- **Moderate (28-32°C):** No shivering, altered mental status, bradycardia
- **Severe (<28°C):** Unconscious, minimal vital signs, appears dead

**Resuscitation Modifications:**
- **Compressions:** May be ineffective; consider withholding if severe bradycardia
- **Medications:** Space at least 10 minutes apart (cold slows metabolism)
- **Defibrillation:** May be ineffective; consider single attempt, then defer
- **Rewarming:** Passive external, active external, or extracorporeal
- **Prognosis:** "Not dead until warm and dead"

**Extracorporeal Rewarming:**
- ECMO or cardiopulmonary bypass
- Most effective for severe hypothermia
- Improves outcomes significantly
- Consider transfer to capable center

**Case Study:** Severe hypothermia with cardiac arrest. Learner must manage appropriately.

---

### Lesson 5.3: Resuscitation in Trauma
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Case Study

Trauma cardiac arrest requires modified resuscitation approach.

**Trauma Cardiac Arrest Causes:**
- Hypovolemia (hemorrhage)
- Tension pneumothorax
- Cardiac tamponade
- Massive head injury
- Spinal cord injury

**Resuscitation Modifications:**
- **Hemorrhage Control:** Identify and control bleeding
- **Airway:** Maintain cervical spine precautions
- **Breathing:** Provide high-flow oxygen, treat pneumothorax
- **Circulation:** Fluid resuscitation, massive transfusion protocol
- **Medications:** Standard ACLS medications
- **Defibrillation:** Standard approach
- **Resuscitative Hysterotomy:** Consider if pregnant

**Resuscitative Thoracotomy:**
- Emergency department procedure
- Indications: Blunt trauma with cardiac arrest, penetrating trauma with signs of life
- Allows pericardiotomy for tamponade, cross-clamping of aorta
- Improves outcomes in selected cases

**Case Study:** Trauma with cardiac arrest. Learner must manage appropriately.

---

### Lesson 5.4: Resuscitation in Pulmonary Embolism
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Case Study

Pulmonary embolism (PE) is a reversible cause of cardiac arrest if recognized and treated.

**PE Recognition:**
- Sudden dyspnea
- Chest pain
- Tachycardia
- Hypotension
- Cardiac arrest (massive PE)

**ECG Changes:**
- Sinus tachycardia
- Right axis deviation
- S1Q3T3 (classic but uncommon)
- Right ventricular strain

**Resuscitation Approach:**
- **CPR:** Standard approach
- **Medications:** Epinephrine, consider thrombolytics
- **Thrombolytics:** Alteplase, reteplase, tenecteplase
- **Embolectomy:** Surgical or catheter-based
- **ECMO:** Consider if available

**Thrombolytic Dosing:**
- **Alteplase:** 0.6 mg/kg IV over 15 minutes (max 50 mg)
- **Reteplase:** 10 units IV, repeat in 30 minutes
- **Tenecteplase:** 0.5-0.6 mg/kg IV (max 50 mg)

**Case Study:** Massive PE with cardiac arrest. Learner must manage appropriately.

---

### Lesson 5.5: Resuscitation in Anaphylaxis
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Case Study

Anaphylaxis can progress to cardiac arrest if not recognized and treated rapidly.

**Anaphylaxis Recognition:**
- Sudden onset
- Respiratory symptoms (wheezing, stridor)
- Cardiovascular symptoms (hypotension, shock)
- Skin symptoms (urticaria, flushing)
- Gastrointestinal symptoms (nausea, vomiting)

**Resuscitation Approach:**
- **Epinephrine:** 0.01 mg/kg IM immediately (max 0.5 mg)
- **CPR:** If cardiac arrest occurs
- **Antihistamines:** After epinephrine
- **Corticosteroids:** After epinephrine
- **Vasopressors:** If hypotension persists
- **Transport:** To hospital immediately

**Biphasic Reaction:**
- Symptoms may recur after initial improvement
- Observe for at least 4-8 hours
- Be prepared for second dose of epinephrine

**Case Study:** Anaphylaxis progressing to cardiac arrest. Learner must manage appropriately.

## Module 6: Special Populations (6 hours)

### Lesson 6.1: Resuscitation in Infants
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Assessment

Infant resuscitation differs from older children due to anatomical and physiological differences.

**Anatomical Differences:**
- Larger head
- Anterior larynx
- Shorter neck
- Larger tongue
- Narrower airway
- Obligate nasal breather

**Physiological Differences:**
- Higher metabolic rate
- Faster respiratory rate
- Faster heart rate
- Lower blood pressure
- Smaller blood volume

**Resuscitation Modifications:**
- **Compressions:** 2 fingers or thumb technique, 1/3 chest depth
- **Ventilation:** Mouth-to-nose or BVM
- **Medications:** Weight-based dosing
- **IV Access:** Intraosseous preferred if IV difficult
- **Defibrillation:** Pediatric pads if available

**Assessment:** Learner must identify infant-specific resuscitation modifications.

---

### Lesson 6.2: Resuscitation in Adolescents
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Assessment

Adolescent resuscitation approaches adult techniques but with weight-based dosing.

**Physiological Considerations:**
- Approaching adult physiology
- Adult-sized equipment usually appropriate
- Weight-based medication dosing
- Consider adult vs. pediatric approach

**Resuscitation Approach:**
- **Compressions:** Two-hand technique, 1/3 chest depth
- **Ventilation:** Mouth-to-mouth or BVM
- **Medications:** Weight-based or adult dosing
- **IV Access:** IV preferred, intraosseous if difficult
- **Defibrillation:** Adult pads and dosing

**Assessment:** Learner must identify adolescent-specific resuscitation modifications.

### Lesson 6.3: Resuscitation in Obesity
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Case Study

Obesity presents unique challenges in resuscitation.

**Challenges:**
- Difficult IV access
- Difficult airway
- Increased oxygen consumption
- Difficult chest compressions
- Difficulty with positioning
- Increased medication requirements

**Modifications:**
- **IV Access:** Consider intraosseous or central line
- **Airway:** Anticipate difficult airway
- **Compressions:** May require additional rescuers
- **Positioning:** Ramped position for intubation
- **Medications:** Consider weight-based dosing
- **Equipment:** Bariatric equipment if available

**Case Study:** Obese patient with cardiac arrest. Learner must manage appropriately.

### Lesson 6.4: Resuscitation in Drowning
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Case Study

Drowning victims may recover with aggressive resuscitation, especially if cold water immersion.

**Drowning Physiology:**
- Laryngospasm prevents water aspiration initially
- Hypoxia leads to loss of consciousness
- Aspiration may occur after loss of consciousness
- Cold water provides neuroprotection

**Resuscitation Approach:**
- **Rescue:** Remove from water safely
- **CPR:** Begin immediately if unresponsive and not breathing
- **Airway:** Manage aspiration with positioning and suctioning
- **Medications:** Standard ACLS medications
- **Rewarming:** If hypothermia present
- **Prognosis:** Aggressive resuscitation warranted

**Case Study:** Drowning victim with prolonged submersion. Learner must manage appropriately.

---

### Lesson 6.5: Resuscitation in Toxicology
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Case Study

Toxin exposure can cause cardiac arrest and requires specific management.

**Common Toxins:**
- **Opioids:** Naloxone
- **Tricyclic Antidepressants:** Sodium bicarbonate
- **Calcium Channel Blockers:** Calcium, vasopressors
- **Beta-Blockers:** Glucagon, vasopressors
- **Organophosphates:** Atropine, pralidoxime
- **Cyanide:** Hydroxocobalamin, sodium thiosulfate

**Resuscitation Approach:**
- **CPR:** Standard approach
- **Medications:** Standard ACLS + specific antidotes
- **Supportive Care:** Oxygen, ventilation, circulation support
- **Poison Control:** Contact for guidance
- **Transport:** To hospital immediately

**Case Study:** Toxin exposure with cardiac arrest. Learner must manage appropriately.

## Module 8: Post-Resuscitation Care (4 hours)

### Lesson 8.1: Targeted Temperature Management
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Assessment

Targeted temperature management improves neurological outcomes after cardiac arrest.

**Indications:**
- ROSC after VF/VT
- ROSC after asystole or PEA (may consider)
- Unconscious after ROSC

**Temperature Targets:**
- Mild hypothermia: 32-34°C
- Normothermia: 36-37.5°C
- Avoid hyperthermia

**Methods:**
- **Passive External Cooling:** Remove blankets, cool environment
- **Active External Cooling:** Ice packs, cooling blankets
- **Active Internal Cooling:** IV cold saline, cooling catheters
- **Extracorporeal Cooling:** ECMO, cardiopulmonary bypass

**Duration:**
- Induction: Rapidly to target temperature
- Maintenance: 12-24 hours at target temperature
- Rewarming: 0.5°C/hour

**Assessment:** Learner must identify appropriate temperature management.

---

### Lesson 8.2: Neurological Prognostication
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Case Study

Predicting neurological outcome after cardiac arrest is important for prognostication and family counseling.

**Prognostic Factors:**
- **Favorable:**
  - Witnessed arrest
  - Bystander CPR
  - Short time to ROSC
  - Shockable initial rhythm
  - Young age
  - Female gender
  - Mild hypothermia

- **Unfavorable:**
  - Unwitnessed arrest
  - No bystander CPR
  - Long time to ROSC
  - Non-shockable initial rhythm
  - Advanced age
  - Comorbidities
  - Severe hypoxia

**Neurological Assessment:**
- Glasgow Coma Scale
- Pupil response
- Corneal reflex
- Gag reflex
- Motor response

**Imaging:**
- CT head: Identify structural lesions
- MRI: Assess ischemic injury (delayed)

**Case Study:** Post-cardiac arrest patient with prognostication. Learner must assess appropriately.

---

### Lesson 8.3: Complications of Resuscitation
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Case Study

Resuscitation can cause complications that require recognition and management.

**Complications:**
- **Thoracic:** Rib fractures, pneumothorax, hemothorax, cardiac contusion
- **Abdominal:** Splenic rupture, hepatic laceration, gastric rupture
- **Neurological:** Anoxic brain injury, stroke, seizures
- **Metabolic:** Acidosis, hyperkalemia, hypoglycemia
- **Infection:** Aspiration pneumonia, sepsis

**Management:**
- Identify complication
- Treat appropriately
- Monitor for complications
- Prevent further injury

**Case Study:** Resuscitation complication scenario. Learner must manage appropriately.

### Lesson 8.4: Organ Donation and End-of-Life Care
**Duration:** 60 minutes | **Type:** Video + Assessment

After unsuccessful resuscitation, organ donation and end-of-life care are important considerations.

**Organ Donation:**
- Identify potential donors
- Notify organ procurement organization
- Maintain organ perfusion
- Support family decision-making
- Facilitate donation process

**End-of-Life Care:**
- Discontinue resuscitation when appropriate
- Provide comfort care
- Support family
- Facilitate grief counseling
- Arrange autopsy if indicated

**Assessment:** Learner must identify appropriate end-of-life care.
