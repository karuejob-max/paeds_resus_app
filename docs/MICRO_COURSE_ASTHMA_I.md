## Course Overview

Paediatric Asthma I teaches recognition and first-hour management of acute severe asthma exacerbations in children. Aligned with GINA 2024 and ISPAD guidelines, focusing on LMIC-safe management with available resources (oxygen, salbutamol, systemic corticosteroids, ipratropium). Designed for providers in resource-limited settings where severe asthma remains a leading cause of paediatric emergency admissions.

---

## Module 1: Recognising Acute Severe Asthma (15 min)

**Learning Objectives:**
- Identify clinical signs of acute asthma exacerbation severity
- Differentiate mild-moderate from severe exacerbations
- Recognise life-threatening asthma (status asthmaticus)
- Understand when to escalate care

**Content:**

Acute asthma exacerbations present with wheezing, cough, and dyspnoea. Severity ranges from mild (child can speak full sentences) to life-threatening (silent chest, altered mental status). Early recognition and rapid treatment prevent progression to status asthmaticus, a medical emergency with high mortality in LMIC settings.

**Severity Classification:**

**Mild-Moderate Exacerbation:**
- Dyspnoea with activity or at rest, but can speak full sentences
- Respiratory rate <50/min (age-adjusted)
- Accessory muscle use: mild or absent
- Peak flow (if available): >50% predicted
- Oxygen saturation: >92% on room air
- Response: Responds to bronchodilators within 1-2 hours

**Acute Severe Asthma:**
- Dyspnoea at rest; speaks in short phrases only
- Respiratory rate 50-60/min (age-adjusted)
- Accessory muscle use: marked (intercostal, subcostal, suprasternal retractions)
- Peak flow (if available): 30-50% predicted
- Oxygen saturation: 90-92% on room air
- Agitation, inability to lie flat
- Requires urgent bronchodilators + systemic corticosteroids

**Life-Threatening Asthma (Status Asthmaticus):**
- Silent chest (no air movement on auscultation)
- Altered mental status (confusion, lethargy, drowsiness)
- Inability to speak (even single words)
- Severe respiratory distress with exhaustion
- Cyanosis (central) or pallor
- Bradycardia (ominous sign; indicates fatigue)
- Hypotension or shock
- Requires immediate ICU-level care, mechanical ventilation

## Module 2: First-Hour Management (20 min)

**Learning Objectives:**
- Administer rapid-acting bronchodilators (salbutamol)
- Initiate systemic corticosteroids
- Provide supplemental oxygen
- Recognise treatment response and failure

**Content:**

**Step 1: Oxygen Therapy**
- **Goal:** SpO2 ≥94% (target 94-98% to avoid hyperoxia)
- **Method:** Nasal cannula (2-4 L/min) or face mask (5-8 L/min)
- **Titrate:** Increase oxygen if SpO2 <94%; reduce if SpO2 >98% to avoid CO2 retention

**Step 2: Rapid-Acting Bronchodilator (Salbutamol/Albuterol)**
- **Route:** Inhaled (preferred) or IV (if severe, unable to inhale)
- **Inhaled dose:** 0.15 mg/kg (max 5 mg) via nebuliser or MDI + spacer
  - Repeat every 15-20 minutes for first hour (up to 3 doses)
  - If continuous nebulisation available: 0.5 mg/kg/hour
- **IV dose (if severe, unable to inhale):** 10 μg/kg bolus over 10 minutes, then infusion 0.1-0.2 μg/kg/min
- **Response:** Improved air entry, reduced retractions, decreased wheezing within 15-30 minutes

**Step 3: Systemic Corticosteroids**
- **Goal:** Reduce airway inflammation; prevent relapse
- **Drug:** Oral prednisolone or IV methylprednisolone
- **Oral prednisolone:** 1-2 mg/kg (max 50 mg) once daily for 5-7 days
- **IV methylprednisolone:** 1-2 mg/kg (max 125 mg) once, then oral prednisolone
- **Timing:** Give within first 30 minutes of presentation
- **Effect:** Peaks at 4-6 hours; reduces relapse risk by 50%

**Step 4: Anticholinergic (Ipratropium)**
- **Indication:** Acute severe asthma; adds benefit to salbutamol
- **Dose:** 0.25-0.5 mg via nebuliser (combined with salbutamol)
- **Frequency:** Every 20 minutes for first hour, then every 4-6 hours
- **Effect:** Synergistic with salbutamol; improves FEV1 by additional 10-15%

**Step 5: Reassessment at 1 Hour**
- **Good response (60-70% of cases):**
  - Improved air entry, reduced wheezing
  - Reduced retractions
  - SpO2 ≥94% on reduced oxygen
  - Able to speak full sentences
  - **Action:** Continue bronchodilators every 2-4 hours; discharge with oral corticosteroids if stable

- **Partial response (20-30% of cases):**
  - Some improvement but still symptomatic
  - Persistent retractions, reduced air entry
  - SpO2 90-94%
  - **Action:** Continue intensive bronchodilators; consider IV magnesium sulphate (see Module 3)

- **Poor response/Deterioration (5-10% of cases):**
  - No improvement or worsening
  - Silent chest, altered mental status, exhaustion
  - SpO2 <90% despite oxygen
  - **Action:** URGENT escalation to ICU; prepare for mechanical ventilation

## Module 3: Escalation and Advanced Management (10 min)

**Learning Objectives:**
- Recognise treatment failure
- Administer IV magnesium sulphate
- Prepare for mechanical ventilation
- Communicate handover (SBAR)

**Content:**

**IV Magnesium Sulphate (for Partial/Poor Response)**
- **Indication:** Acute severe asthma not responding to initial therapy
- **Dose:** 25-50 mg/kg (max 2 g) IV over 15-20 minutes
- **Mechanism:** Smooth muscle relaxant; bronchodilator effect
- **Effect:** Improves FEV1 by 10-15%; reduces ICU admission by 15-20%
- **Monitoring:** Check for hypotension, flushing, headache

**Recognition of Status Asthmaticus (Escalation Trigger)**
- Silent chest (no air movement)
- Altered mental status
- Inability to speak
- Severe respiratory distress with exhaustion
- Bradycardia or hypotension
- **Action:** Immediate transfer to ICU; notify anaesthesia for intubation

**Pre-Transfer Actions**
- Continue oxygen, salbutamol, corticosteroids
- Establish IV access (2 lines if possible)
- Have emergency medications ready (epinephrine, atropine)
- Document all interventions and times
- Prepare for manual ventilation if transport delayed

**Handover (SBAR)**
- **Situation:** "8-year-old with acute severe asthma, not responding to initial therapy"
- **Background:** "Presented 2 hours ago with dyspnoea, wheezing. Given salbutamol + ipratropium + prednisolone. Now has silent chest, altered mental status."
- **Assessment:** "Status asthmaticus; imminent respiratory failure. SpO2 85% on high-flow oxygen."
- **Recommendation:** "Needs ICU-level care with mechanical ventilation. Can you accept transfer?"

## Quiz (10 Questions, 80% Pass)

1. **A 6-year-old with acute asthma has dyspnoea at rest, speaks in short phrases, RR 55/min. What severity is this?**
   - A) Mild-moderate exacerbation
   - B) Acute severe asthma
   - C) Life-threatening asthma
   - D) Chronic asthma
   - **Correct: B**

2. **What is the first-line drug for acute asthma exacerbation?**
   - A) Oral prednisolone
   - B) Salbutamol (albuterol)
   - C) Theophylline
   - D) Leukotriene inhibitor
   - **Correct: B**

3. **Salbutamol dose for acute asthma is 0.15 mg/kg. For a 20 kg child, what is the dose?**
   - A) 1.5 mg
   - B) 3 mg
   - C) 5 mg
   - D) 10 mg
   - **Correct: B** (0.15 × 20 = 3 mg)

4. **When should systemic corticosteroids be given in acute asthma?**
   - A) Only if no response to bronchodilators
   - B) Within first 30 minutes of presentation
   - C) After 1 hour of observation
   - D) Only in life-threatening asthma
   - **Correct: B**

5. **Which finding indicates life-threatening asthma (status asthmaticus)?**
   - A) Wheezing with tachycardia
   - B) Retractions with dyspnoea
   - C) Silent chest with altered mental status
   - D) Cough with fever
   - **Correct: C**

6. **What is the role of ipratropium in acute asthma?**
   - A) Replaces salbutamol
   - B) Synergistic with salbutamol; improves bronchodilation
   - C) Prevents relapse
   - D) Treats infection
   - **Correct: B**

7. **A child has partial response to salbutamol + corticosteroids at 1 hour. What is the next step?**
   - A) Discharge home
   - B) Continue intensive bronchodilators; consider IV magnesium sulphate
   - C) Start antibiotics
   - D) Perform chest X-ray
   - **Correct: B**

8. **IV magnesium sulphate dose is 25-50 mg/kg. For a 10 kg child, what is the dose range?**
   - A) 25-50 mg
   - B) 100-200 mg
   - C) 250-500 mg
   - D) 500-1000 mg
   - **Correct: C** (25-50 × 10 = 250-500 mg)

9. **Which sign indicates imminent respiratory failure in asthma?**
   - A) Tachycardia
   - B) Bradycardia
   - C) Tachypnoea
   - D) Fever
   - **Correct: B** (Bradycardia = fatigue, ominous sign)

10. **What oxygen saturation target should you aim for in acute asthma?**
    - A) 85-90%
    - B) 90-92%
    - C) 94-98%
    - D) 100%
    - **Correct: C**
