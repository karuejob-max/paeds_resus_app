## Course Overview

Septic Shock II builds on Septic Shock I recognition and first-hour actions, focusing on **fluid-refractory shock management**, **catecholamine selection and titration**, **advanced monitoring**, and **safe escalation to tertiary care**. Designed for providers managing refractory cases where initial fluid boluses and basic interventions have not achieved adequate perfusion.

---

## Module 1: Recognising Fluid-Refractory Shock (20 min)

**Learning Objectives:**
- Define fluid-refractory shock (persistence of hypoperfusion signs after 40-60 mL/kg crystalloid)
- Identify clinical signs of ongoing shock despite fluid resuscitation
- Understand risk factors for fluid-refractory progression
- Recognise when to escalate to vasoactive support

**Content:**

### 1.1 Definition and Epidemiology
- **Fluid-refractory shock:** Hypoperfusion persisting after 40-60 mL/kg crystalloid over first 1 hour
- **Incidence:** 15-30% of septic shock cases in LMIC settings; higher in severe malaria, severe anaemia, severe malnutrition
- **Mortality impact:** Fluid-refractory shock carries 3-5× higher mortality if not escalated promptly

### 1.2 Clinical Recognition (Bedside Assessment)
- **Persistent signs despite fluid boluses:**
  - Capillary refill time >2 seconds (or >3 seconds in dark skin)
  - Weak or absent peripheral pulses
  - Altered mental status (lethargy, confusion)
  - Urine output <0.5 mL/kg/hour
  - Lactate >4 mmol/L (if available) or persistent elevation after first bolus

- **Shock classification (WHO/ISPAD):**
  - **Compensated shock:** Tachycardia, tachypnoea, cool extremities, normal BP
  - **Uncompensated shock:** Hypotension, altered mental status, weak pulses
  - **Refractory shock:** Uncompensated despite fluids, requires inotropes/vasopressors

### 1.3 Pathophysiology of Fluid Refractory Shock
- **Distributive failure:** Vasodilation + capillary leak (cytokine-mediated)
- **Cardiogenic component:** Myocardial depression from sepsis (TNF-α, IL-1)
- **Hypovolaemia + maldistribution:** Fluid moves to interstitium; central vessels remain underfilled
- **Microcirculatory dysfunction:** Capillary plugging, endothelial dysfunction
- **Consequence:** Fluid alone cannot restore perfusion; vasoactive drugs needed

### 1.4 Risk Factors for Progression to Fluid-Refractory Shock
- **Severe infection source:** Meningitis, necrotising fasciitis, severe pneumonia
- **Host factors:** Severe malnutrition, severe anaemia, immunosuppression
- **Delayed recognition:** Shock not identified until late (>2 hours from symptom onset)
- **Inadequate initial fluid:** <40 mL/kg in first hour
- **Comorbidities:** Chronic illness, previous sepsis

### 1.5 When to Escalate: Decision Points
- **After 40-60 mL/kg crystalloid:** Reassess perfusion
- **If signs persist:** Start vasoactive support (do NOT delay waiting for perfect conditions)
- **If hypotension develops:** Immediate vasopressor initiation
- **If lactate remains elevated:** Consider inotropic support

## Module 2: Vasoactive Drugs and Titration (25 min)

**Learning Objectives:**
- Understand pharmacology of epinephrine, norepinephrine, and dopamine in septic shock
- Calculate and titrate vasoactive infusions safely
- Recognise appropriate drug choice based on shock phenotype
- Manage complications (arrhythmias, tissue ischaemia)

**Content:**

### 2.1 Vasoactive Drug Selection (ISPAD/AHA Guidance)
- **First-line inotrope/vasopressor:** Epinephrine (adrenaline)
  - **Dose:** 0.05-1.0 μg/kg/min IV/IO infusion
  - **Mechanism:** α1 (vasoconstriction) + β1 (inotropic) + β2 (mild vasodilation at low doses)
  - **Onset:** 2-5 minutes; titrate every 5-10 minutes
  - **Goal:** Restore perfusion (CRT <2s, pulses palpable, urine output, mental status)

- **Alternative: Norepinephrine** (if available, preferred in some settings)
  - **Dose:** 0.05-1.0 μg/kg/min IV/IO infusion
  - **Mechanism:** Primarily α1 (vasoconstriction) + β1 (mild inotropic)
  - **Advantage:** Less tachycardia than epinephrine; preferred in some adult guidelines
  - **Availability:** Often unavailable in LMIC; use epinephrine if norepinephrine not stocked

- **Dopamine** (historical; not first-line in paediatric septic shock)
  - **Dose:** 5-20 μg/kg/min IV/IO infusion
  - **Mechanism:** Dose-dependent (low: renal; medium: inotropic; high: vasopressor)
  - **Limitation:** Unpredictable effects in sepsis; increased arrhythmia risk
  - **Use only if:** Epinephrine/norepinephrine unavailable

### 2.2 Preparation and Administration
- **Concentration:** Epinephrine 1:10,000 (0.1 mg/mL) for IV/IO infusion
  - Dilute in normal saline or 5% dextrose to desired concentration (e.g., 1 mg in 50 mL = 20 μg/mL)
- **Access:** Central line preferred (peripheral acceptable if central unavailable; monitor for extravasation)
- **Rate calculation:**
  - **Formula:** (Drug concentration in μg/mL) × (Desired dose in μg/kg/min) × (Weight in kg) / 60 = mL/hour
  - **Example:** 5 kg child, epinephrine 0.1 μg/kg/min, concentration 20 μg/mL
    - (20 × 0.1 × 5) / 60 = 0.17 mL/hour (use infusion pump)

### 2.3 Titration Strategy
- **Start low, titrate up:** Begin at 0.05-0.1 μg/kg/min; increase every 5-10 minutes
- **Target perfusion endpoints:**
  - Capillary refill time <2 seconds
  - Palpable pulses (radial, femoral)
  - Urine output >0.5 mL/kg/hour
  - Normal mental status (alert, responsive)
  - Lactate trending down (if available)
- **Typical effective dose:** 0.1-0.5 μg/kg/min; rarely >1 μg/kg/min needed
- **Reassess every 5-10 minutes** during titration

### 2.4 Monitoring During Infusion
- **Vital signs:** HR, BP (if available), RR, SpO2 every 5 minutes during titration
- **Perfusion:** CRT, pulses, urine output, mental status
- **Complications:**
  - **Tachycardia >180 bpm:** May indicate inadequate perfusion or excessive epinephrine; reassess
  - **Arrhythmias:** Rare in paediatric sepsis; if occur, reduce dose and check for hypokalaemia
  - **Hypertension:** Unusual in septic shock; if occurs, reduce dose
  - **Extravasation (peripheral line):** Tissue blanching, pain; stop infusion, elevate limb, consider phentolamine infiltration if available

### 2.5 Transition to Maintenance
- **Once perfusion restored:** Continue infusion at effective dose
- **Wean gradually:** Only after shock resolved and underlying infection controlled (antibiotics, source control)
- **Weaning strategy:** Reduce by 10-20% every 30-60 minutes if perfusion maintained

## Module 3: Advanced Monitoring and Escalation (15 min)

**Learning Objectives:**
- Interpret basic monitoring (lactate, urine output) to assess shock progression
- Recognise when to escalate to tertiary care
- Communicate handover effectively (SBAR)
- Manage complications (acute kidney injury, DIC)

**Content:**

### 3.1 Monitoring Markers of Shock Resolution
- **Lactate (if available):**
  - **Normal:** <2 mmol/L
  - **Elevated:** >4 mmol/L indicates tissue hypoxia
  - **Trend:** Falling lactate = improving perfusion; rising lactate = worsening shock
  - **Interpretation:** Lactate >4 despite vasoactive support = consider escalation

- **Urine output:**
  - **Goal:** >0.5 mL/kg/hour
  - **Measurement:** Catheterise if possible; measure hourly
  - **Oliguria (<0.5 mL/kg/hour) despite fluids + inotropes:** Risk of acute kidney injury; escalate

- **Repeated clinical assessment:**
  - **Perfusion:** CRT, pulses, skin colour
  - **Mental status:** Alertness, response to commands
  - **Vital signs:** HR, BP (if available), RR

### 3.2 Recognition of Refractory Shock (Escalation Triggers)
- **Persistent hypoperfusion despite 40-60 mL/kg fluid + epinephrine 0.5-1.0 μg/kg/min:**
  - Indicates need for advanced support (ICU, advanced inotropes, mechanical support)
- **Oliguria with rising creatinine:** Acute kidney injury; risk of hyperkalemia
- **Bleeding manifestations (petechiae, purpura, oozing):** DIC; risk of multi-organ failure
- **Altered mental status worsening:** Cerebral hypoperfusion; urgent escalation needed

### 3.3 Escalation Pathway and Handover (SBAR)
- **Situation:** "5-year-old with suspected meningococcal sepsis, in refractory shock"
- **Background:** "Presented 3 hours ago with fever, rash, lethargy. Given 60 mL/kg fluid bolus. Now on epinephrine 0.3 μg/kg/min but still has CRT >2s, weak pulses."
- **Assessment:** "Fluid-refractory shock; likely meningitis. Lactate 6 mmol/L. Urine output minimal."
- **Recommendation:** "Needs ICU-level care: advanced inotropes, mechanical ventilation, possible ECMO consideration. Can you accept transfer?"

- **Pre-transfer actions:**
  - Continue current therapy (fluids, epinephrine, antibiotics)
  - Secure airway if GCS <8
  - Establish second IV/IO line if possible
  - Document all interventions and times

### 3.4 Complications During Escalation
- **Acute kidney injury (AKI):**
  - **Risk:** Oliguria + hypotension + sepsis
  - **Management:** Maintain perfusion, avoid nephrotoxins, monitor K+
  - **Dialysis:** If available at tertiary centre

- **Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC):**
  - **Signs:** Bleeding (petechiae, oozing), thrombocytopenia, prolonged PT/INR
  - **Management:** Treat underlying infection, transfuse platelets/FFP if available, avoid further trauma

- **Myocardial depression:**
  - **Sign:** Hypotension despite high-dose epinephrine + normal fluid status
  - **Management:** Escalate to ICU for advanced support (milrinone, ECMO)

## Quiz (12 Questions, 80% Pass)

1. **A 6-year-old with septic shock has received 50 mL/kg crystalloid. CRT remains >2s, pulses weak. What is the next step?**
   - A) Give another 30 mL/kg fluid bolus
   - B) Start epinephrine infusion (0.05-0.1 μg/kg/min)
   - C) Observe for 30 minutes
   - D) Refer to paediatric ICU without intervention
   - **Correct: B**

2. **Epinephrine 0.1 μg/kg/min is ordered for a 4 kg child. Concentration is 20 μg/mL. What is the infusion rate in mL/hour?**
   - A) 0.12 mL/hour
   - B) 0.33 mL/hour
   - C) 1.2 mL/hour
   - D) 3.3 mL/hour
   - **Correct: A** (20 × 0.1 × 4 / 60 = 0.13 ≈ 0.12)

3. **Which of the following indicates fluid-refractory shock?**
   - A) Tachycardia after 20 mL/kg fluid
   - B) Persistent CRT >2s after 40-60 mL/kg fluid
   - C) Fever with normal perfusion
   - D) Tachypnoea with normal oxygen saturation
   - **Correct: B**

4. **A child on epinephrine 0.3 μg/kg/min has HR 185 bpm. What should you do?**
   - A) Increase epinephrine to 0.5 μg/kg/min
   - B) Reassess perfusion; if adequate, continue current dose; if inadequate, increase
   - C) Stop epinephrine immediately
   - D) Give additional fluid bolus
   - **Correct: B**

5. **Lactate is 6 mmol/L after 60 mL/kg fluid + epinephrine 0.4 μg/kg/min. What does this indicate?**
   - A) Normal response to treatment
   - B) Persistent tissue hypoxia; consider escalation
   - C) Epinephrine overdose
   - D) Fluid overload
   - **Correct: B**

6. **Which drug is first-line for paediatric septic shock?**
   - A) Dopamine
   - B) Milrinone
   - C) Epinephrine
   - D) Dobutamine
   - **Correct: C**

7. **A child receiving epinephrine via peripheral IV shows blanching at the infusion site. What is the most likely complication?**
   - A) Infection
   - B) Extravasation
   - C) Thrombophlebitis
   - D) Air embolism
   - **Correct: B**

8. **When should you escalate a child with septic shock to ICU?**
   - A) Immediately on diagnosis
   - B) After 2 hours of therapy
   - C) If hypoperfusion persists despite 40-60 mL/kg fluid + vasoactive support
   - D) Only if bleeding develops
   - **Correct: C**

9. **Which perfusion endpoint is NOT a target for shock resolution?**
   - A) CRT <2 seconds
   - B) Urine output >0.5 mL/kg/hour
   - C) Heart rate >150 bpm
   - D) Palpable pulses
   - **Correct: C**

10. **A 3-year-old in septic shock has oliguria (urine output 0.2 mL/kg/hour) despite fluids + epinephrine. What is the risk?**
    - A) Hyperkalemia
    - B) Acute kidney injury
    - C) Both A and B
    - D) Neither
    - **Correct: C**

11. **Which statement about norepinephrine in paediatric septic shock is TRUE?**
    - A) It is the first-line agent in all LMIC settings
    - B) It causes more tachycardia than epinephrine
    - C) It is preferred if available; epinephrine is alternative
    - D) It should never be used in children
    - **Correct: C**

12. **A child's lactate is falling (6 → 4 mmol/L) after 2 hours of therapy. What does this indicate?**
    - A) Worsening shock
    - B) Improving perfusion; continue current therapy
    - C) Need to increase epinephrine
    - D) Readiness to wean vasoactive support
    - **Correct: B**
