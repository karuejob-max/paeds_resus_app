# SECTION 1: THE FINANCIAL PHILOSOPHY

### 1.1  The Constitutional Revenue Principle

***Individual provider access to Care Signal, ResusGPS, and core courses is never gated by ability to pay in any setting where Paeds Resus operates. Revenue comes from institutions, governments, and funders who benefit from the intelligence the platform produces. Providers who contribute observations are not charged for the contribution. This principle is non-negotiable and may not be overridden by commercial pressure.***

This principle exists because the providers who need this platform most are in the lowest-income settings and can afford it least. AHA certifications at current Kenyan pricing already represent a significant personal financial burden for nurses and clinical officers. Scaling a model built entirely on individual provider payments would price out the population with the highest child mortality burden.

The model that works at global scale without paywalling the providers who need it most is institutional and governmental. Hospitals, ministries, insurers, and global health funders pay for the intelligence the platform produces. Individual providers access the core platform free or at subsidised cost. The Learning System's intelligence — which is produced by those individual providers' observations — is what institutional buyers are purchasing.

### 1.2  The Governing Analogy

The closest financial analogue to what Paeds Resus is building is not a SaaS company or an NGO. It is the aviation safety ecosystem. Airlines pay for safety certification, route audits, maintenance standards, and training compliance because the evidence base that informs all of it was generated by pilots and air traffic controllers who reported near-misses and incidents at no personal cost. The reporting is free. The intelligence and the certification that flows from it is what generates revenue.

Paeds Resus operates the same logic: providers report into Care Signal and Safe-Truth at no cost. The Failure Pattern Knowledge Base that emerges from those reports becomes the basis for institutional subscriptions, accreditation, and policy consultation. The providers who generated the intelligence share in its value through better clinical guidance, better training content, and a system that has genuinely learned from their experiences.

### 1.3  What Paeds Resus Will Not Do

- Paywall Care Signal reporting for any provider in any setting
- Paywall ResusGPS bedside guidance in settings where providers cannot afford it
- Publicly rank hospitals by emergency readiness for patient or family use — the ethical exposure is unresolvable and the incentive alignment is wrong
- Sell individual provider data to third parties for commercial purposes
- Build bespoke consulting engagements that trade leadership time for money — only productised, fixed-scope institutional offerings
- Make accreditation claims before the Knowledge Base is mature enough to make them credible

# SECTION 2: THE FOUR BUSINESSES

Paeds Resus contains four mutually reinforcing businesses. They are not alternatives — they are sequential and interdependent. Each stage funds the next and makes it more credible.

| Business | What it is and how it feeds the others |
|---|---|
| 1. Education | AHA certifications, micro-courses, and Fellowship. Individual provider payments. Generates cash flow now. Also generates the provider workforce and the observations that feed the Learning System. |
| 2. Quality Improvement | Productised ERS packages, institutional readiness audits, premium analytics. Institutional revenue. Generates the implementation evidence the Learning System needs to evaluate its own recommendations. |
| 3. Learning Network | Intelligence licensing, peer-learning facilitation, policy consultation, annual knowledge reports. The platform's deepest moat. Powered by Education observations and QI implementation records. |
| 4. Decision Support | ResusGPS pathways updated by confirmed knowledge, delivered at the point of care. In markets where clinical decision support is reimbursed or procured as a health system tool, a distinct premium revenue stream. |

Business 3 makes all others better. Business 1 generates the observations Business 3 needs. Business 2 generates the implementation evidence Business 3 needs to evaluate its recommendations. Business 4 delivers what Business 3 has learned back to the bedside, which generates more observations, completing the cycle.

# SECTION 3: BUSINESS 1 — EDUCATION

Education is the current primary revenue engine and the right foundation. It is transactional, predictable, and directly tied to product value delivered. It is also the entry point through which providers join the platform and begin contributing to the Learning System.

### 3.1  AHA Certification Revenue

| Course | Current pricing (KES) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| BLS | 7,500 per participant | All clinical cadres |
| ACLS | 17,500 per participant | Doctors and senior nurses |
| BLS + ACLS Combined | 25,000 per participant | Recommended; ~30% bundle discount |
| PALS | To be confirmed | Paediatric emergency providers |
| NRP | To be confirmed | Neonatal emergency providers |
| Heartsaver | To be confirmed | Non-clinical and allied health |
| Instructor Course | To be confirmed | Issues Instructor Number |

Institutional group rates apply to structured cohort-based training. The Nyeri County Referral Hospital model — cohort organised by a named coordinator, payment deadline one week before assessment, next-cohort clause for those who miss the deadline — is the template for all institutional AHA delivery.

Instructor development is a high-leverage revenue multiplier: a trained Paeds Resus Instructor can deliver AHA training on institutional schedules under Paeds Resus oversight, generating certification revenue at scale without requiring direct CEO delivery time. Instructor development should be prioritised in every institutional ERS partnership.

### 3.2  Micro-Course Revenue

Condition-specific micro-courses are individually priced certifications. Each covers one high-burden paediatric emergency condition. Course II for any condition requires Course I completion.

Pricing model: individual micro-course certifications priced at a level accessible to LMIC providers on personal income, with institutional bulk access available through the Quality Improvement subscription tier. The individual price point should be set so that a registered nurse in Kenya can afford one course per month without employer subsidy.

The micro-course catalog currently includes: Paediatric Septic Shock I and II, Paediatric Asthma I and II, Convulsive Status Epilepticus I, and 24 further conditions. Each course completed generates one more provider who understands a specific condition more deeply and is more likely to recognise it early — which is the Learning System's most direct mechanism of impact.

### 3.3  Paeds Resus Fellowship Revenue

The Fellowship is a 24-month discipline pathway. It is not a fast track. It is not a title that can be purchased. Its value derives from what it requires: sustained engagement, consistent Care Signal reporting, and documented ResusGPS use across 15 conditions.

Revenue model: the Fellowship carries an enrollment fee covering the 24-month pathway. Individual course and certification fees within the Fellowship may be discounted for enrolled Fellows. The Fellowship title, once earned, is a verified credential that providers can display professionally — distinct from AHA certifications, and the only credential in paediatric emergency care that requires demonstrated reflective practice over time, not only knowledge at a point in time.

The Fellowship is also the primary driver of Care Signal reporting volume: Pillar C requires one eligible submission per calendar month for 24 consecutive months. Every Fellow in active Fellowship is a guaranteed monthly Care Signal contributor. At 100 active Fellows, that is 100 Care Signal submissions per month minimum. At 1,000 active Fellows, 1,000 per month. This is the mechanism by which the Education business directly feeds the Learning System.

*Fellowship enrollment is the highest-value individual revenue event on the platform and the highest-value observation-generation commitment. These two facts are not in tension. They are the same fact.*

### 3.4  Education Revenue Ceiling and Why It Is Not the Long-Term Engine

Education revenue scales with user acquisition. User acquisition in LMIC settings faces price sensitivity, employer support variability, and the cost of the constitutional revenue principle — providers who cannot afford it must not be excluded. These factors create a ceiling on education revenue as a proportion of total revenue.

The ceiling is not a reason to abandon education revenue — it is the right revenue source for the current stage. It is a reason to build Business 2 and Business 3 in parallel, so that the constitutional revenue principle can be funded by institutional and governmental revenue rather than by individual providers who can least afford it.

# SECTION 4: BUSINESS 2 — QUALITY IMPROVEMENT

Quality Improvement is the institutional revenue engine. It generates recurring income from hospitals, health systems, and NGOs. It also generates the implementation evidence the Learning System needs to evaluate whether its own recommendations work.

### 4.1  The Productised ERS Package

The Hospital Emergency Readiness System (ERS) is Paeds Resus's primary institutional offering. It is not bespoke consulting. It is a fixed-scope, fixed-deliverable package with a defined implementation timeline and an annual recurring review fee.

| Component | What is included |
|---|---|
| Baseline Readiness Audit | Structured assessment against the Paeds Resus Emergency Readiness Standard: ERT on call 24/7, ResusGPS deployed, emergency trolley stocked and checked, Care Signal reporting rate above threshold, training coverage above threshold. Gap report with prioritised action items. |
| ResusGPS Deployment | Platform access for all clinical staff at the facility. Onboarding session. Clinical champion identified and supported. |
| Care Signal Embedding | Facility registered on platform. Staff onboarded. Reporting culture established. Monthly reporting rate tracked from month one. |
| AHA Training Cohort | Structured BLS/ACLS/PALS delivery for a defined cohort of facility staff. Instructor development where eligible. |
| Institutional Dashboard Access | Real-time view of Care Signal reporting rate, ResusGPS adoption, training coverage, and gap closure progress for facility leadership. |
| Annual Review | 12-month reassessment against the readiness standard. Gap trend analysis. Benchmark comparison against similar facilities. Recommendations for year two. |

Pricing model: the ERS package is priced per facility per year. The baseline implementation has a one-time setup fee. Annual review is a recurring subscription. Pricing is tiered by facility level: Level 4 (sub-county hospital), Level 5 (county referral), Level 6 (national referral) each at different price points reflecting the institutional budget reality and the scale of the implementation.

The ERS package is designed so that a facility purchasing it cannot purchase a placebo therapy. The reporting rate target, the training coverage threshold, and the annual review cycle ensure that the package either changes the system or the gap is made visible. This is what differentiates it from a seat-count training purchase.

### 4.2  Premium Analytics for Institutional Subscribers

Institutions that are registered on the platform and contributing Care Signal data receive access to facility-level analytics as a standard feature. Premium analytics — available as an add-on subscription — provides:

- Failure pattern trend reports: how the facility's most common failure domains have changed month-over-month over the subscription period
- Benchmark comparisons: anonymous comparison of the facility's reporting rates, gap distributions, and temporal interval metrics against similar facilities on the platform
- Predictive gap alerts: when a facility's Care Signal data shows an emerging pattern that matches a Confirmed or Established Failure Pattern in the Knowledge Base, leadership is alerted before the pattern becomes critical
- Board-level readiness reports: quarterly summaries formatted for hospital leadership and board presentation, covering readiness status, training coverage, gap trends, and action items
- Peer-learning recommendations: when a Validated Success Pattern from a similar facility is available and relevant to the facility's current gap profile, the platform surfaces it (with the originating facility's consent)

Premium analytics is the first product where the Learning System's intelligence becomes directly billable. Its value depends entirely on the density and quality of the Care Signal dataset. It is not viable until the dataset contains enough observations from enough facilities to make the comparisons and pattern detections meaningful. Target: 10+ facilities contributing Care Signal data consistently for 6+ months before premium analytics is launched.

### 4.3  CNE and CPD Institutional Delivery

Continuing Nursing Education (CNE) and Continuing Professional Development (CPD) events delivered at the institutional level represent a recurring revenue opportunity. Hospitals and professional associations have budgets for CPD that do not face the same price sensitivity as individual provider spending.

The model: Paeds Resus designs and delivers structured CPD events — simulation days, case review sessions, resuscitation update workshops — for institutional clients on a per-event or annual retainer basis. These events are designed to feed back into the platform: participants are onboarded to ResusGPS and Care Signal as part of the event, so the CPD event generates new platform users and new observation contributors.

# SECTION 5: BUSINESS 3 — LEARNING NETWORK

The Learning Network is the most strategically important business and the hardest to build. It is the business that no competitor can replicate without also building the dataset, the Adaptive Learning System, and the institutional relationships simultaneously. It is a three-to-five year horizon from today.

### 5.1  Intelligence Licensing

The Failure Pattern Knowledge Base — once sufficiently populated — is the only resource of its kind in the world: a longitudinal, multi-site dataset of paediatric emergency failure modes and success patterns, classified by domain, linked to outcomes, accumulated from real clinical events in LMIC settings.

This dataset is valuable to: ministries of health (national surveillance, training priority setting, procurement planning); global health funders (evidence for investment decisions, programme evaluation); researchers (implementation science, clinical epidemiology, intervention evaluation); equipment manufacturers designing products for LMIC contexts; WHO and regional health bodies (policy development, guideline adaptation for low-resource settings).

Intelligence licensing model: annual access agreements with defined data products — annual knowledge reports, pattern prevalence datasets (anonymised and aggregated), implementation evidence summaries. Priced at the level of a research partnership or a surveillance contract, not a software subscription. The first intelligence licensing conversations should begin when the Knowledge Base contains confirmed patterns from at least three countries and 1,000+ observations.

### 5.2  The Peer-Learning Model

This is the most strategically original revenue model in the portfolio. When the Knowledge Base contains Validated Success Patterns — practices that have measurably improved outcomes at specific facilities — Paeds Resus can facilitate their transfer to other facilities facing the same failure patterns.

The mechanism: facility A has a Confirmed Failure Pattern in IO access delay. The Knowledge Base contains a Validated Success Pattern from facility B showing that storing IO kits at triage reduced access time by 60%. With facility B's consent, Paeds Resus facilitates a structured knowledge transfer: the specific protocol, the implementation sequence, the training approach, and the outcome metrics. Facility A receives a targeted implementation package. Facility B is recognised as a learning exemplar.

Revenue model: peer-learning facilitation is charged as a consultancy fee to facility A. Facility B receives recognition and, where appropriate, a revenue share. The platform takes a facilitation fee. This model generates revenue, strengthens institutional relationships, and produces Implementation records that feed back into the Learning System — building evidence about which success patterns transfer well across contexts.

***The peer-learning model requires explicit, purpose-specific consent from the sharing facility before any identification or protocol sharing occurs. Facility identity is never disclosed without consent. The platform never implies that facility B is superior to facility A — it facilitates specific practice transfer, not institutional ranking.***

### 5.3  Ministry and Government Partnerships

At sufficient scale, Paeds Resus's Care Signal and Safe-Truth dataset becomes a national paediatric emergency surveillance asset. Ministries of health currently have limited visibility into what is actually failing in paediatric emergency care at the facility level. Mortality registers capture outcomes but not failure modes. Paeds Resus can offer what no other organisation currently provides: operational intelligence about where, how, and why children are dying from preventable causes.

Government partnership model: a structured data-sharing agreement in which Paeds Resus provides anonymised, aggregated national-level pattern intelligence to the Ministry of Health in exchange for: formal endorsement of the platform for facility use, inclusion in national CPD frameworks, support for Care Signal adoption as a standard QI reporting mechanism, and a partnership fee that funds the analytics infrastructure.

This is not a charity partnership. It is a value exchange: the Ministry receives intelligence it cannot generate itself; Paeds Resus receives institutional legitimacy that accelerates adoption and the reporting volume that makes the intelligence more valuable. Both sides benefit. The partnership fee reflects that.

### 5.4  Annual Knowledge Report

The Annual Knowledge Report is a standalone intelligence product: a published summary of what the Adaptive Learning System has learned in the preceding year. Confirmed failure patterns, their prevalence and trend direction, validated success patterns, implementation evidence, and unresolved questions. It is not a marketing document. It is a scientific report.

Revenue model: licensed to institutions, ministries, and global health funders as a premium subscription. Free executive summary available publicly to build awareness and credibility. Full dataset available to research partners under data access agreements. The annual report is also the primary mechanism by which Paeds Resus maintains scientific credibility — publishing findings with appropriate uncertainty labelling, acknowledging where the evidence is insufficient, and updating its own positions when the data demands it.

# SECTION 6: BUSINESS 4 — DECISION SUPPORT

Decision Support is what ResusGPS becomes when it is continuously updated by confirmed knowledge from the Adaptive Learning System. At that point it is no longer education. It is clinical decision support — a distinct product category with distinct regulatory considerations and a distinct value proposition.

### 6.1  The Decision Support Value Proposition

A ResusGPS pathway that has been updated based on a Confirmed Failure Pattern from 200 facilities in three countries is delivering the collective learning of those facilities to the next provider who opens the app. That is qualitatively different from a guideline-based protocol written by a committee and published once. It is living clinical guidance, continuously improved by real clinical events.

The value proposition to institutions: deploying ResusGPS gives your clinical staff access to guidance that improves based on what similar facilities are learning. The longer you deploy it and the more your staff contribute to Care Signal, the better the guidance becomes for your facility specifically.

The value proposition to governments and health systems: a single procurement of ResusGPS access across a network of facilities creates a platform that learns from the network and returns improved guidance to every facility simultaneously. The more facilities contribute, the better the guidance for all.

### 6.2  Regulatory Considerations

ResusGPS currently positions as reference support and clinical decision aid, not as a medical device. This positioning must be maintained with discipline as the platform scales. The moment ResusGPS is characterised as autonomous diagnostic or prescriptive software, it enters a regulatory category that requires formal medical device approval in most jurisdictions.

The correct characterisation: ResusGPS is a structured clinical reference tool that supports trained providers in applying established guidelines. Every recommendation is traceable to a published guideline or a Knowledge Stewardship-approved platform finding. The provider retains full clinical responsibility. The platform does not diagnose, prescribe, or replace professional judgment.

As the platform expands internationally, regulatory counsel in each jurisdiction must review the positioning before institutional procurement is agreed. This is a legal requirement, not an option.

### 6.3  Decision Support Revenue Model

In markets where clinical decision support tools can be procured by governments or reimbursed by insurers as a health system investment, ResusGPS access becomes a distinct line item in institutional budgets. This is different from the ERS package (which includes training, audit, and dashboard access) — it is specifically the value of the continuously updated clinical guidance itself.

Revenue model: annual per-facility or per-provider access to ResusGPS at the institutional procurement level. Priced differently from individual provider access (which remains free or subsidised under the constitutional revenue principle) — the institutional procurement covers the facility's deployment, integration support, and access to the platform's knowledge update cycle.

# SECTION 7: LONG-TERM — ACCREDITATION

Accreditation is the most strategically powerful long-term revenue model and the one that requires the most patience to pursue correctly. It is a five-to-ten year horizon.

### 7.1  The Accreditation Model

Paeds Resus Emergency Readiness Accreditation: a formal certification that a facility has met a defined, transparent, publicly documented standard of paediatric emergency readiness. Renewable annually. Based on the Paeds Resus Emergency Readiness Standard (a document to be developed from the ERS audit criteria and the Knowledge Base).

The revenue mechanism: facilities seek accreditation because it unlocks something valuable — insurance contracting, government procurement eligibility, staff retention, and institutional reputation. The accreditation is not purchased; it is earned by meeting the standard and verified by audit.

The precedent: Joint Commission International (JCI) accreditation in the USA, COHSASA in Africa. Both generate substantial recurring revenue from annual accreditation fees and renewal audits. Both require decades of credibility-building before the accreditation means enough to institutions to be worth pursuing.

### 7.2  What Must Be True Before Accreditation Is Launched

- The Emergency Readiness Standard must be published and peer-reviewed — not an internal document but a published, publicly available standard with clinical governance sign-off
- The Failure Pattern Knowledge Base must be mature enough to ground the standard in evidence — the criteria must be demonstrably connected to outcomes, not to process measures alone
- The audit methodology must be independent enough to be credible — Paeds Resus cannot be both the standard-setter and the sole auditor without creating a conflict of interest; independent audit partners must be developed
- At least 20 facilities must have completed a full ERS package cycle with documented outcome improvements — accreditation without a track record is a certificate of payment, not a certificate of quality
- Legal counsel must have reviewed the accreditation scheme in each jurisdiction for liability, regulatory compliance, and consumer protection requirements
- Insurance and government procurement conversations must have confirmed that accreditation status is a relevant consideration in their contracting decisions — an accreditation nobody uses is worthless

### 7.3  Insurance Integration

Insurance integration follows accreditation, not precedes it. The model: Paeds Resus certifies that a hospital has met a defined emergency readiness standard. Insurers use that certification as one factor — among many — in contracting and premium decisions. Paeds Resus never makes the contracting decision. It provides the readiness evidence.

This is ethically clean because: the standard is transparent and published; the auditing process is defined and consistent; the accreditation is binary (accredited / not yet accredited), not ordinal; and Paeds Resus has no financial interest in the insurer's contracting outcome beyond the accreditation fee itself.

# SECTION 9: FINANCIAL SUSTAINABILITY PRINCIPLES

### 9.1  The Revenue-Mission Alignment Test

Every revenue stream must pass a single test: does generating this revenue make the Adaptive Learning System better, or does it dilute it? This test resolves most apparent tensions between revenue and mission.

| Revenue stream | Does it make the Learning System better? |
|---|---|
| AHA certifications | Yes — each certified provider is a potential Care Signal contributor and ResusGPS user. The training generates the workforce that generates the observations. |
| Fellowship fees | Yes — each enrolled Fellow commits to 24 months of monthly Care Signal reporting. Fellowship revenue directly funds the observation volume the Learning System needs. |
| ERS package fees | Yes — each institutional ERS client generates facility-level Care Signal data, implementation records, and outcome data that are the Learning System's most valuable inputs. |
| Premium analytics subscriptions | Yes — institutions that pay for analytics have a financial incentive to improve their Care Signal reporting quality and volume, which improves the analytics, which justifies the subscription. |
| Intelligence licensing | Yes — licensing revenue funds the analytics infrastructure and the Knowledge Stewardship function that makes the intelligence credible. |
| Individual subscription paywalling Care Signal | No — reduces reporting volume, degrades the Learning System, reduces the value of everything else. This is why the constitutional revenue principle exists. |
| Bespoke consulting that trades CEO time for money | No — does not scale, does not generate platform data, and removes leadership attention from the Learning System architecture. |

### 9.2  The Subsidised Access Model

The constitutional revenue principle requires that providers who cannot afford individual access are not excluded. The subsidised access model:

- Individual providers in settings identified as resource-constrained receive free or heavily subsidised access to ResusGPS and Care Signal. The subsidy is funded by institutional revenue from the same or comparable settings.
- Micro-course certifications are individually priced at the lowest viable point for LMIC individual income. Institutional bulk access — where employers pay — funds the individual subsidy.
- Fellowship enrollment is individually priced. Bursary pathways are available for providers who cannot self-fund, funded from institutional partner contributions and donor grants.
- AHA certifications are individually priced at current Kenyan market rates. Institutional cohort pricing provides a group discount. Employer-funded cohorts are the primary delivery model; individual self-funding is available but not the assumed pathway.

The goal is to make individual provider exclusion based on inability to pay a policy failure that is visible and addressable, not an invisible structural feature of the business model.

### 9.3  Grant and Donor Funding

Grant and donor funding is appropriate for specific, time-limited purposes that advance the platform toward commercial sustainability. It is not a long-term revenue strategy and it is not a substitute for institutional revenue.

Appropriate uses: Knowledge Base infrastructure development (a public good that commercial revenue cannot yet fund); bursary programmes for Fellowship enrollment in under-resourced settings; first-mover institutional partnerships where the subsidy enables the data generation that makes subsequent commercial partnerships viable; and research partnerships that generate peer-reviewed evidence for the platform's impact claims.

Inappropriate uses: operational expenses that should be covered by commercial revenue; marketing; product development that serves only the funded partner and not the broader platform mission.

Grant strategy: target funders whose interests align with the Adaptive Learning System — global health implementation funders, health system strengthening programmes, child survival research funds. The pitch is not "fund our training programme." It is "fund the instrument that will generate evidence about why children are dying from preventable causes and how to stop it."

# SECTION 10: WHAT FINANCIAL SUCCESS LOOKS LIKE

### 10.1  The 18-Month Financial Milestone

- AHA certification delivery is generating consistent monthly revenue across at least three institutional cohorts simultaneously
- At least two institutional ERS packages are in active implementation with signed annual review agreements
- Fellowship enrollment has reached a cohort size where monthly Care Signal submissions from active Fellows exceed 200 per month
- Micro-course catalog covers all 15 fellowship conditions with individual and institutional pricing active
- The premium analytics product is in design with a defined data density target for launch
- A bursary pathway for Fellowship enrollment is funded and has supported at least five providers who could not self-fund

### 10.2  The Five-Year Financial Position

At five years, the revenue mix should have shifted materially from its current composition. Education revenue (Business 1) should represent no more than 40% of total revenue. Quality Improvement revenue (Business 2) should represent 40-50%. Learning Network revenue (Business 3) should represent 10-20% and growing. This shift reflects the maturing Knowledge Base and the increasing value of the intelligence it produces.

The five-year test is not a revenue target. It is a revenue mix test: if after five years, Paeds Resus is still primarily an education business, the Adaptive Learning System has not yet produced the institutional value that justifies its existence. The revenue mix is a proxy for how much of the platform's value is being extracted from the Learning System versus the training catalogue.

### 10.3  The Financial Failure Modes

- Becoming a certification mill: revenue from certifications that do not change behaviour, funded by providers who cannot afford them, generating no observation data for the Learning System
- Becoming a consulting business: bespoke engagements that generate revenue from leadership time rather than from platform scale, with no learning system data generated and no path to scale
- Paywalling the mission: restricting Care Signal or ResusGPS access to generate individual subscription revenue, reducing the observation volume that makes everything else valuable
- Premature accreditation: launching an accreditation scheme before the standard is credible, the audit methodology is independent, or the market has confirmed that the accreditation is worth pursuing
- Grant dependency: operating costs funded primarily by donor grants with no path to commercial sustainability, making the platform vulnerable to funding cycles and donor priorities
