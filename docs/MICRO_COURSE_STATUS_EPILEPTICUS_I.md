## Course Overview

Paediatric Status Epilepticus I teaches recognition and first-hour management of acute seizures and status epilepticus in children. Aligned with ILAE 2015 and ISPAD guidelines, focusing on LMIC-safe management with available resources (benzodiazepines, phenytoin, phenobarbital). Designed for providers managing seizures in resource-limited settings where status epilepticus remains a leading cause of paediatric mortality if untreated.

## Module 1: Recognising Acute Seizures and Status Epilepticus (15 min)

**Learning Objectives:**
- Identify clinical signs of acute seizures (generalised, focal, absence)
- Define status epilepticus (continuous seizure >5 minutes or recurrent without recovery)
- Recognise life-threatening seizures
- Understand when to escalate care

**Content:**

Acute seizures present with abnormal motor activity, altered consciousness, or autonomic changes. Status epilepticus is a medical emergency with high mortality if untreated. Early recognition and rapid anticonvulsant therapy prevent progression to refractory status epilepticus and death.

**Seizure Classification:**

**Generalised Tonic-Clonic Seizure:**
- Loss of consciousness
- Tonic phase: Rigid muscle contraction (10-20 seconds)
- Clonic phase: Rhythmic jerking (20-60 seconds)
- Post-ictal: Confusion, drowsiness (minutes to hours)
- Duration: Usually <2 minutes

**Focal Seizure (with or without secondary generalisation):**
- Localised jerking or twitching (one limb, face)
- May progress to generalised seizure
- Consciousness may be preserved (simple focal) or lost (complex focal)

**Absence Seizure:**
- Brief loss of consciousness (5-10 seconds)
- Staring, no response to commands
- Rapid recovery, no post-ictal confusion
- Multiple episodes per day (typical absence)

**Status Epilepticus:**
- **Definition:** Continuous seizure ≥5 minutes OR recurrent seizures without recovery of consciousness between episodes
- **Early status:** 5-30 minutes (still responsive to first-line drugs)
- **Established status:** >30 minutes (increased risk of refractory status)
- **Refractory status:** Continues despite 2+ anticonvulsants (high mortality)

**Life-Threatening Seizure:**
- Prolonged seizure (>10 minutes)
- Status epilepticus
- Repeated seizures without recovery
- Aspiration risk (altered consciousness, secretions)
- Hypoxia, hyperthermia, rhabdomyolysis
- **Action:** Immediate anticonvulsant therapy

## Module 2: First-Hour Management (20 min)

**Learning Objectives:**
- Administer first-line benzodiazepines
- Initiate second-line anticonvulsants
- Provide supportive care (airway, oxygen)
- Recognise treatment response and failure

**Content:**

**Step 1: Airway and Breathing**
- **Position:** Recovery position (left lateral) to prevent aspiration
- **Airway:** Clear secretions; insert airway adjunct if needed
- **Oxygen:** Provide if SpO2 <94% or signs of hypoxia
- **Suction:** Gently suction mouth/pharynx if secretions present

**Step 2: First-Line Benzodiazepine (0-5 minutes)**
- **Goal:** Stop seizure within 5 minutes
- **Drug options:**
  - **Lorazepam (preferred):** 0.1 mg/kg IV/IO (max 4 mg) over 1-2 minutes
  - **Diazepam:** 0.2-0.5 mg/kg IV/IO (max 10 mg) over 2-3 minutes
  - **Midazolam (if IV/IO unavailable):** 0.2-0.3 mg/kg IM (max 10 mg)
- **Response:** Seizure stops within 1-2 minutes in 60-80% of cases
- **Repeat:** If seizure continues after 5 minutes, give second dose

**Step 3: Second-Line Anticonvulsant (5-20 minutes)**
- **Indication:** If seizure continues after 2 benzodiazepine doses
- **Drug options:**
  - **Phenytoin:** 15-20 mg/kg IV (max 1000 mg) over 15-20 minutes
    - Slower onset (10-20 minutes) but longer duration (24 hours)
    - Risk of arrhythmias; monitor cardiac rhythm
  - **Phenobarbital:** 15-20 mg/kg IV (max 1000 mg) over 10-15 minutes
    - Slower onset (15-30 minutes) but very long duration (48-72 hours)
    - Risk of respiratory depression; have bag-valve-mask ready
  - **Levetiracetam:** 20-30 mg/kg IV (max 1500 mg) over 5-10 minutes
    - Faster onset (5-10 minutes); fewer drug interactions
    - Preferred if available

**Step 4: Reassessment at 20 Minutes**
- **Good response (70-80% of cases):**
  - Seizure stopped
  - Regaining consciousness
  - Breathing spontaneously, SpO2 >94%
  - **Action:** Continue monitoring; identify seizure cause (fever, infection, hypoglycaemia)

- **Partial response (10-15% of cases):**
  - Seizure reduced but not stopped
  - Recurrent brief seizures
  - **Action:** Give third anticonvulsant or escalate to ICU

- **No response/Refractory Status (5-10% of cases):**
  - Seizure continues despite 2+ anticonvulsants
  - Altered consciousness, respiratory depression
  - **Action:** URGENT escalation to ICU; prepare for mechanical ventilation

## Module 3: Escalation and Refractory Status Management (10 min)

**Learning Objectives:**
- Recognise refractory status epilepticus
- Initiate advanced management (continuous infusions)
- Prepare for mechanical ventilation
- Communicate handover (SBAR)

**Content:**

**Refractory Status Epilepticus (RSE)**
- **Definition:** Seizure continues despite 2+ anticonvulsants
- **Incidence:** 5-10% of status epilepticus cases
- **Mortality:** 20-40% if untreated
- **Triggers:** Severe infection (meningitis), metabolic (hypoglycaemia), structural (stroke)

**Advanced Management (ICU-Level)**
- **Continuous benzodiazepine infusion:**
  - Lorazepam: 0.05-0.1 mg/kg/hour IV infusion
  - Midazolam: 0.1-0.2 mg/kg/hour IV infusion
- **Continuous second-line infusion:**
  - Phenobarbital: 1-5 mg/kg/hour IV infusion
  - Propofol: 1-3 mg/kg/hour IV infusion (ICU only)
- **Mechanical ventilation:** Required if respiratory depression or inability to protect airway

**Pre-Transfer Actions**
- Continue benzodiazepine infusion
- Establish IV access (2 lines if possible)
- Have emergency medications ready (additional anticonvulsants, vasopressors)
- Document all interventions and times
- Prepare for manual ventilation if transport delayed

**Handover (SBAR)**
- **Situation:** "5-year-old with status epilepticus, not responding to initial therapy"
- **Background:** "Presented 1 hour ago with seizure. Given lorazepam + phenytoin. Now has recurrent seizures, altered consciousness."
- **Assessment:** "Refractory status epilepticus; imminent respiratory failure. Likely meningitis (fever, neck stiffness)."
- **Recommendation:** "Needs ICU-level care with mechanical ventilation and continuous anticonvulsant infusions. Can you accept transfer?"

## Quiz (10 Questions, 80% Pass)

1. **How is status epilepticus defined?**
   - A) Any seizure lasting >1 minute
   - B) Continuous seizure ≥5 minutes or recurrent without recovery
   - C) Multiple seizures in one day
   - D) Seizure with loss of consciousness
   - **Correct: B**

2. **What is the first-line drug for acute seizure?**
   - A) Phenytoin
   - B) Benzodiazepine (lorazepam, diazepam)
   - C) Phenobarbital
   - D) Levetiracetam
   - **Correct: B**

3. **Lorazepam dose for status epilepticus is 0.1 mg/kg. For a 10 kg child, what is the dose?**
   - A) 0.1 mg
   - B) 1 mg
   - C) 4 mg
   - D) 10 mg
   - **Correct: B** (0.1 × 10 = 1 mg)

4. **When should a second-line anticonvulsant be given?**
   - A) Immediately with benzodiazepine
   - B) If seizure continues after 5 minutes (2 benzodiazepine doses)
   - C) Only if status epilepticus confirmed
   - D) After 30 minutes of observation
   - **Correct: B**

5. **Which finding indicates refractory status epilepticus?**
   - A) Seizure lasting >5 minutes
   - B) Seizure continuing despite 2+ anticonvulsants
   - C) Altered consciousness during seizure
   - D) Fever with seizure
   - **Correct: B**

6. **What is the risk of phenytoin infusion?**
   - A) Respiratory depression
   - B) Hypotension
   - C) Cardiac arrhythmias
   - D) Hypoglycaemia
   - **Correct: C**

7. **A child has seizure lasting 8 minutes. What is the first action?**
   - A) Observe and wait
   - B) Give benzodiazepine immediately
   - C) Perform lumbar puncture
   - D) Start antibiotics
   - **Correct: B**

8. **Phenobarbital dose is 15-20 mg/kg. For a 15 kg child, what is the dose range?**
   - A) 15-20 mg
   - B) 75-100 mg
   - C) 225-300 mg
   - D) 1000 mg
   - **Correct: C** (15-20 × 15 = 225-300 mg)

9. **What is the post-ictal period?**
   - A) Time during seizure
   - B) Time after seizure when consciousness is regaining (confusion, drowsiness)
   - C) Time before seizure starts
   - D) Time between seizures
   - **Correct: B**

10. **Which position is safest for a seizing child?**
    - A) Supine
    - B) Prone
    - C) Recovery position (left lateral)
    - D) Sitting upright
    - **Correct: C**
