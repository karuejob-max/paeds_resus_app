## Module 1: Recognition of Septic Shock (15 min)

**Learning objectives:**
- Define septic shock in observable, bedside terms
- Identify clinical signs of shock (perfusion, pulse, CRT, breathing, responsiveness)
- Distinguish septic shock from other shock types (hypovolemic, cardiogenic)
- Recognize when a child is "sick enough to worry"

**Content:**

### What is septic shock?

Septic shock is **infection + body's response = inadequate perfusion**. At the bedside, you see:

- **Poor perfusion:** Cold extremities, delayed capillary refill (>2 seconds), weak pulses
- **Altered mental status:** Lethargy, irritability, confusion (child not responding normally)
- **Tachycardia:** Heart rate elevated for age
- **Tachypnea:** Breathing fast (compensating for metabolic acidosis)
- **Fever or hypothermia:** Often fever, but hypothermia is a bad sign

### Clinical signs you can see and feel

| Sign | What to look for |
|------|------------------|
| **Perfusion** | Skin warm or cold? Extremities pink or pale/mottled? |
| **Capillary refill** | Press fingernail bed for 3 seconds, release. Does color return in <2 sec? |
| **Pulse** | Strong or weak? Easy to feel or hard to find? |
| **Breathing** | Fast? Labored? Grunting? |
| **Mental status** | Alert and playful, or sleepy/confused? |
| **Urine output** | Wet diapers? Last void when? |

### Red flags for septic shock

- **Fever + lethargy** = sick child, not just "fever"
- **Cold extremities + fever** = shock (bad sign)
- **Weak pulse + tachycardia** = body trying hard, failing
- **Altered mental status** = brain not getting enough oxygen
- **Breathing fast + working hard** = body compensating

### Why early recognition matters

In low-resource settings, you may be the **first and only** person to see this child. Early recognition → early treatment → child lives. Delay → organ failure → death.

## Module 2: First-Hour Safe Actions (15 min)

**Learning objectives:**
- Establish IV/IO access safely
- Give fluids correctly (bolus volume, reassessment)
- Start antibiotics if available
- Recognize when to escalate (call for help)

**Content:**

### The first hour: What you do saves lives

**Goal:** Get fluids in, antibiotics started, escalate to senior/referral. Do this in the first 60 minutes.

### Step 1: Establish IV or IO access

**IV access (preferred if fast):**
- Use largest bore needle you can place safely
- Peripheral IV in arm or leg
- If you can place it in <2 minutes, do it
- If you cannot find a vein in 2 attempts, move to IO

**IO access (intraosseous, if IV fails):**
- Needle into bone marrow (tibia or humerus)
- Fast, reliable, works for fluids and drugs
- If trained, use this if IV fails
- If not trained, ask senior staff or escalate

**Why it matters:** You need access to give fluids fast. Without it, the child dies.

### Step 2: Give fluids (bolus)

**Bolus volume:** 20 mL/kg of **balanced crystalloid** (Ringer's lactate preferred, normal saline acceptable)

**Example:** 10 kg child = 200 mL bolus

**How to give:**
- Push fluids fast (over 15-20 minutes if possible)
- Watch the child: Is perfusion improving? Pulse stronger? Skin warmer?
- After bolus: Reassess

**Reassess after first bolus:**
- Is perfusion better? (Warmer skin, stronger pulse, better mental status?)
- **YES** → Give second bolus if still signs of shock
- **NO** → Stop fluids, escalate NOW (child needs drugs, not more fluid)

**Why this matters:** Too much fluid too fast can hurt; too little fluid and shock continues. You must reassess.

### Step 3: Start antibiotics (if available)

**Empiric antibiotics for septic shock (first-line in most settings):**
- **Ceftriaxone** 50-80 mg/kg IV/IM (max 2g per dose)
- **PLUS Gentamicin** 7.5 mg/kg IV/IM (max 500mg per dose)
- **OR Ampicillin** 50 mg/kg IV/IM (max 2g per dose) + Gentamicin (if ceftriaxone unavailable)

**If penicillin allergy:**
- **Chloramphenicol** 25 mg/kg IV/IM (max 1g per dose)
- **PLUS Gentamicin**

**Timing:** Give antibiotics **within the first hour**. Do not delay for tests.

**Why this matters:** Antibiotics kill the infection. Every hour of delay increases death risk.

### Step 4: Recognize when to escalate

**Escalate (call senior/refer) if:**
- After 2 boluses (40 mL/kg fluid), child still has signs of shock
- Child has altered mental status (very sleepy, not responding)
- Child is in respiratory distress (working hard to breathe, grunting)
- You do not have IV/IO access
- You do not have antibiotics
- Child is a newborn or <2 months old (higher risk)

**What to say when escalating:**
"I have a child with fever, cold extremities, weak pulse, and altered mental status. I've given [X mL] fluid and started antibiotics. I need help now."

## Module 3: When to Refer (10 min)

**Learning objectives:**
- Identify when a child needs hospital-level care
- Communicate clearly with referral center
- Arrange safe transport

**Content:**

### Septic shock is a hospital emergency

Your facility may not have everything needed (vasoactive drugs, ICU, blood transfusion). Recognize when to refer.

### Refer to hospital if:

- Child needs **vasoactive drugs** (dopamine, adrenaline) — you don't have them
- Child has **refractory shock** (still in shock after 2 boluses + antibiotics)
- Child has **respiratory failure** (can't breathe on own, needs ventilator)
- Child has **altered mental status** (encephalopathy)
- You suspect **meningitis** (stiff neck, rash, altered mental status)
- Child is **newborn or <2 months** (high-risk group)

### How to refer safely

**Before transport:**
- Give fluids and antibiotics (as above)
- Keep child warm (wrap in blanket)
- Monitor breathing and pulse during transport
- If child stops breathing, start CPR

**Communication with hospital:**
- Call ahead: "Septic shock, [age], [weight], given [fluid volume] and antibiotics, ETA [time]"
- Send written summary: Vital signs, fluids given, antibiotics given, clinical findings
- Ensure someone trained accompanies child

**Transport:**
- Use safest, fastest route to hospital
- If long distance, consider calling ambulance
- Monitor child continuously

## Module Quiz (10 questions, 80% pass threshold)

**Question 1:** A 5-year-old with fever has cold extremities, weak pulse, and is very sleepy. What is the first thing you do?
A) Give antibiotics only
B) Establish IV access and give 20 mL/kg fluid bolus
C) Refer to hospital immediately
D) Give antipyretics (paracetamol)

**Answer:** B (Establish IV access and give 20 mL/kg fluid bolus)

**Question 2:** After giving one 20 mL/kg bolus, the child still has cold extremities and weak pulse. What do you do?
A) Give another 20 mL/kg bolus
B) Stop fluids and escalate
C) Give antibiotics only
D) Wait 1 hour and reassess

**Answer:** A (Give another 20 mL/kg bolus, then reassess)

**Question 3:** What is the maximum dose of ceftriaxone for a 10 kg child?
A) 500 mg
B) 1 g
C) 2 g
D) 4 g

**Answer:** C (2 g is the max per dose)

**Question 4:** A child has septic shock but you cannot place an IV. What is your next step?
A) Give antibiotics IM only
B) Establish IO access
C) Refer immediately
D) Wait for senior staff

**Answer:** B (Establish IO access)

**Question 5:** After 2 boluses (40 mL/kg) and antibiotics, a child still has signs of shock. What do you do?
A) Give a third bolus
B) Escalate to senior/refer to hospital
C) Give vasoactive drugs
D) Continue fluids for 2 more hours

**Answer:** B (Escalate to senior/refer to hospital)

**Question 6:** A 3-month-old has fever and poor perfusion. Is this child high-risk?
A) No, all children respond the same to sepsis
B) Yes, neonates and infants <2 months are high-risk
C) Only if the child is male
D) Only if the child has diarrhea

**Answer:** B (Yes, neonates and infants <2 months are high-risk)

**Question 7:** What does "reassess after bolus" mean?
A) Check vital signs 1 hour later
B) Look for improvement in perfusion, pulse, mental status
C) Give another bolus automatically
D) Stop all treatment

**Answer:** B (Look for improvement in perfusion, pulse, mental status)

**Question 8:** A child with septic shock has a rash and stiff neck. What do you suspect?
A) Malaria
B) Meningitis
C) Dehydration
D) Asthma

**Answer:** B (Meningitis)

**Question 9:** What is the first antibiotic choice for septic shock in most settings?
A) Penicillin alone
B) Ceftriaxone + Gentamicin
C) Chloramphenicol alone
D) Amoxicillin

**Answer:** B (Ceftriaxone + Gentamicin)

**Question 10:** A child is in septic shock and needs a hospital. What should you communicate to the referral center?
A) Just the child's name and age
B) Vital signs, fluids given, antibiotics given, clinical findings
C) Nothing, just send the child
D) Only the diagnosis

**Answer:** B (Vital signs, fluids given, antibiotics given, clinical findings)
